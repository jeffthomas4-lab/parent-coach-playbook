# Parent Coach Playbook: Monetization and Marketing Plan

**Date:** May 2026
**Premise:** Site is content-complete enough to monetize. Question is what makes money without consuming the time you've allocated to Chain Reaction and the football season.

---

## The voice problem is already solved

You built this as a faceless brand. Field & Forge Press is the imprint. PCP Editors is the default byline. Jeff Thomas only appears on cornerstone pieces. Nothing about marketing this brand requires you to post about parenting on your coaching channels or speak about youth sports under your name.

So the real question isn't "how do I market without diluting my voice." It's "how do I market a brand that isn't me, on time I'm not spending on football or the books." That's a different problem and it has cleaner answers.

Stop worrying about voice dilution. The architecture already prevents it.

---

## The AI saturation problem is mostly not your problem

AI is gutting organic search for commodity questions. "How do I throw a curveball." "What size baseball glove for an 8-year-old." Google answer boxes and ChatGPT eat that traffic before you ever see a click.

Your content isn't that. Your content is scenario-plus-script. "The dad who corners coach in the parking lot." "The 7am coach text." "What you say in the first 90 seconds." AI generalizes; you specify. AI gives surveys; you give directives. The work is harder to commodify because it requires opinion, scene, and a defensible voice.

The real risk isn't AI eating your content. It's AI eating your discovery channel. Google traffic is going to fall whether your content is good or not. The play is to stop depending on Google and build channels that survive the shift.

That means email. That means destinations people type in. That means showing up where the audience already gathers.

## Monetization stack, ranked

Built for the constraint that you are not full-time on this. Each tier assumes the one above is working.

### 1. Productize the existing library (build now, low effort)

You already have ~100 articles. Bundle three of them into paid PDFs and sell on Gumroad. Suggested first three:

- **The Drive Home Playbook** — the cornerstone bundle. 30-40 pages. Pull together the post-game conversation pieces, the "am I any good" piece, the bench reaction, the cheer that embarrasses your kid. $19.
- **The Tryouts and Cuts Survival Guide** — pre-tryout dinner, how to read the drill, when the cuts list goes up, the 24-hour rule, the kid who refuses to go. Time it for August publication. $14.
- **The Travel Ball Decision Guide** — the 11-year-old who's not ready, when a friend's family goes travel, the playing time formula, quitting cleanly, the politics. $19.

Realistic Q3 2026 revenue if list is 1,000 subscribers: 50-150 sales total across the three at a 3-5% conversion rate. $700-2,500. Not life-changing. The point is you have an ascending offer ladder behind the lead magnet, and the unit economics get better as the list grows.

### 2. Fix the affiliate mix (build now, no new content needed)

Amazon Associates pays 3-4% on sporting goods. Specialty programs pay 8-12% and convert better because the buyer intent is higher. Replace gear links to the highest-margin partners you can find:

- BSN Sports affiliate program (8%)
- Baseball Express / Softball.com affiliate program (8-10%)
- Source for Sports (varies by region)
- ShareASale has dozens of youth sports retailers in the same vertical

Bigger payoff: affiliate partnerships outside gear. Camp finder platforms, lessons-marketplace platforms (CoachUp, TeamSnap Pay-to-Play), video tools parents and youth coaches actually use (CoachNow, GameChanger, MaxOne). Some of these pay $20-50 per signup, not 8% of a glove. The "how to evaluate a summer camp" article is sitting on top of a $50-per-lead opportunity if you wire it right.

### 3. Sponsored newsletter slots (start charging at 5,000 subscribers)

Once the Kit list is over 5K, you can sell a sponsor slot for $300-500 per send. Newsletters in the parenting / kids-activities space charge that range routinely. Don't sell from a Jeff Thomas voice. Sell from "Parent Coach Playbook reaches X engaged parents weekly." Sponsor doesn't need to know who runs it.

Below 5K, this is not a real channel. Above 10K, it's $1,000-2,000/mo on autopilot.

### 4. Lead-generation deals (test in fall 2026)

Sport-specific local clubs, baseball academies, and travel programs pay $50-200 per qualified parent lead. Your site already collects intent — someone reading "how to evaluate a summer camp" is exactly the buyer those programs want. Pick one regional partner per sport in 2026, run a paid placement, and see what they're willing to pay for an introduction. This works at smaller list sizes than newsletter sponsorships because the leads are higher-value.

### 5. Skip these for now

- No paid membership. Requires a community manager you don't have.
- No course. Same problem. Plus the brand voice is "we tell you what to say in 90 seconds," not "sit through 6 modules."
- No book yet. The PDFs are the book in pieces. Ship those, see what sells, then assemble the bestseller into a Field & Forge Press paperback in late 2027.
- No personal-brand monetization. Don't put your face on it. The whole structural play is that you don't have to.

## Marketing stack, ranked

Filter: low time investment, no exposure of you as Jeff Thomas the coach, channels that survive AI killing search.

### 1. Reddit and Facebook groups (free, requires consistency not creativity)

This is the most underused channel for this audience. r/youthsports, r/baseballparents, r/coachinghighschool, r/Mommit (sport threads), sport-specific subreddits, plus the dozens of large Facebook travel-ball groups by sport and region. Parents are already there asking exactly the questions your articles answer.

Don't spam. Post valuable answers under a brand handle (not your personal account), occasionally linking back when relevant. Even one good Reddit answer per week, written in the brand voice, builds a footprint search and AI can't kill because it's owned by the platform's algorithm, not Google's.

This can be delegated to a VA at $20-30/hour, 2-3 hours/week. Or it's a Cowork session every Monday morning where you batch a week of replies and posts.

### 2. Pinterest (free, evergreen, the most underrated parenting channel)

Pinterest is parents searching for solutions, saving for later, returning to act. The articles on packing lists, snack rotations, gear-by-age, tournament weekend logistics, and car kits are tailor-made for it. Each article needs one vertical pin image; the pin lives forever and accumulates traffic for years. Most parenting bloggers underinvest here because it's not exciting. That's the opportunity.

Cost: a few hours one-time to make a pin template. Then 10-15 minutes per article to push a pin. Or delegate.

### 3. Cross-promotion with parenting / sports newsletters (free, high signal)

Find five newsletters in adjacent spaces — youth sports parenting, kids' development, family logistics — and propose a swap. They mention you, you mention them. No money changes hands. No personal brand required. Builds the list at zero CAC.

Worth one focused afternoon to identify candidates and send the pitches.

### 4. Guest essays in parenting publications (low frequency, high authority)

Outside, Romper, Today's Parent, The Athletic, plus regional parenting magazines. Pitch 4-6 pieces a year under "PCP Editors" or under a contributor pseudonym, not under Jeff Thomas. Each placement drives a small spike to the lead magnet and gives the brand permanent third-party authority. The key is the byline doesn't have to be you.

### 5. One paid ad test (paid, finite spend, real data)

Meta ads to the Drive Home Playbook lead magnet. $500 test budget. Targeting: parents 30-50, sports interests, kids' activities. The metric that matters is cost per email subscriber. Under $3 means the channel works and you can scale. Over $5 means kill it. This is a one-week test, not a campaign you have to run.

If it works, this is how the list grows from 1K to 10K without any time investment from you beyond the initial setup.

### 6. Don't bother with

- TikTok or Instagram Reels. Wrong format for the brand. Massive time sink even when delegated.
- Twitter/X. Wrong audience.
- A podcast. Don't.
- Speaking at parenting conferences. Off-strategy per My Company.

## The 90-day move

If you do nothing else in Q3 2026, do these:

1. **June:** Ship the first paid PDF (The Drive Home Playbook on Gumroad, $19). Wire it into the post-newsletter-signup sequence as the immediate ascending offer.
2. **June:** Audit and replace 20 highest-traffic affiliate links with higher-paying programs. One Cowork session.
3. **July:** Run the $500 Meta ad test to the lead magnet. Measure cost per subscriber.
4. **July:** Stand up the Reddit / Facebook posting cadence (delegated, 2-3 hours/week of someone else's time).
5. **August:** Ship the second PDF (Tryouts and Cuts Survival Guide), timed to back-to-school tryout season.
6. **September:** Pitch one lead-generation deal to a regional camp or academy. Real terms, real dollars per lead.

That's the plan. Six concrete moves, none of them require you to post under your name, none of them require new website features, none of them eat your manuscript time more than a couple of Cowork sessions per month.

## Decisions only you can make

A few things I won't fill in for you:

1. **What's "monetization works" mean to you.** $500/mo by year-end? $2K/mo? Or is this a "build the system now, harvest in 2027" play where revenue in 2026 isn't really the point? The answer changes whether to spend $500 testing ads in July or wait.
2. **Are you willing to hire a VA at $200-400/month** to run the Reddit/Facebook/Pinterest cadence? Without that, the marketing plan compresses to whatever you can do in monthly Cowork sessions, which is real but slower.
3. **Do you want this site to ever carry your name publicly,** or stay faceless permanently? Permanently faceless is fine and probably right. But if there's any future where you put the Jeff Thomas byline on the cornerstone pieces and own the brand publicly, the marketing options expand. Worth deciding now, not later.
