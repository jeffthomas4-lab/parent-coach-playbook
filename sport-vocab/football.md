# Football — sport vocabulary

This file covers tackle football, flag-football, and 7v7. The first half (the field, scoring, downs) applies to all three. The second half is rule-specific — read the section that matches the piece's tag, especially for flag-football where "tackle" is forbidden.

## The field (all variants)

- Correct: gridiron, field, end zone, end line, sideline, goal line, midfield (the 50), hash marks, line of scrimmage, neutral zone, red zone, the flat, the seam, the box (the area near the line of scrimmage)
- Field length: 100 yards plus two 10-yard end zones. Youth fields may be shorter — cite the league.
- Wrong for this sport: pitch, diamond, court, rink

## Players (tackle football)

- Offense: quarterback (QB), running back (RB), fullback (FB), wide receiver (WR), tight end (TE), offensive line (OL — left tackle / LT, left guard / LG, center / C, right guard / RG, right tackle / RT)
- Defense: defensive line (DE, DT, NT), linebackers (LB — middle / MLB, outside / OLB), defensive backs (cornerback / CB, safety / S, free safety / FS, strong safety / SS)
- Special teams: kicker (K), punter (P), long snapper (LS), returner

## Action verbs (tackle football)

- Snap, hand off, pass, throw, catch, run, block, tackle, juke, cut, scramble, sack, intercept, fumble, recover, blitz, rush, cover, route-run, drop back, hand off, play action, pitch (the QB pitching to a running back — narrow technical use), kneel, spike (to stop the clock — narrow technical use), punt, kick
- Routes: slant, post, corner, out, in, comeback, go, fade, screen, drag, dig, wheel
- Wrong for this sport: dribble, dunk, dig, set, body-check, cradle

## Scoring (tackle football, flag-football, 7v7 mostly aligned)

- Touchdown (6), extra point / PAT (1 — kick), two-point conversion (2 — run or pass), field goal (3), safety (2). Flag-football and 7v7 leagues often modify these. Cite the league.
- "The team scored a touchdown." Not "scored a goal," not "scored a basket."
- Wrong for this sport: run (the noun), goal, basket, kill

## Game structure (all variants)

- Down (1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th), yards to gain, drive, possession, quarter (four), half, halftime, overtime
- Wrong for this sport: inning, period, set, match

---

## Flag-football specifics

- **Never write "tackle" in a flag-football piece.** The defender pulls the flag, grabs the flag, removes the flag, or stops the runner. The play ends when the flag is pulled.
- No blocking with the body in most rule sets. Screening, shielding, or routes are acceptable; "blocking" is usually prohibited. Cite the league rules.
- No diving, no stiff-arming the flag-puller in most rule sets.
- Field is often shorter and narrower (40 by 25 yards is common).
- The QB usually has a 7-second pass clock or no rushing of the QB before a count.
- Equipment: flag belt with flags (typically 2 or 3 flags), no pads, no helmet.
- Cross-sport pitfalls: writing "the linebacker tackled the running back" in a flag-football drill — wrong. Pulled the flag.

## 7v7 specifics

- 7-on-7 is passing only. No running plays in most rule sets, no offensive linemen, no contact. The quarterback throws on every play.
- Defense covers; there is no rushing the QB. The QB has a count (often 4 seconds) before the rush is implied or before the play is dead.
- No pads, no helmets in most leagues. Cleats only.
- The ball-carrier is "down" when touched (one or two hands) — the league rule book defines whether it's one-touch or two-hand-touch. Cite.
- Cross-sport pitfalls: writing "the offensive line picked up the blitz" in a 7v7 piece — wrong. There is no offensive line in 7v7. Writing "the running back broke the tackle" — wrong. There is no tackle in 7v7.

## Cross-sport pitfalls (most common slips, all football variants)

- "Scored a goal" — wrong. A touchdown.
- "The basket" — wrong. The end zone.
- "The pitcher" — wrong. The quarterback.
- "The pitch" — wrong as a field reference. The field, the gridiron, the line of scrimmage. Note: "pitch" in football has one narrow technical meaning (the QB pitching to a running back), so use it carefully.
- "Spike" — in football, "spike" means slamming the ball to stop the clock. Don't conflate with volleyball spikes.
