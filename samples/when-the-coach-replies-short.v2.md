It was a Wednesday in late October. We had spent the better part of an hour at the kitchen table drafting an email to our kid's coach. We had a real concern — playing time, but framed politely, the way we'd want a parent to frame it if we were the one coaching. We re-read it twice. We softened one sentence. We checked the punctuation. We hit send at 6:14pm and went to start dinner.

At 6:23pm the reply came in.

> Thanks, will keep an eye on it.

That was the whole thing. Five words and a comma.

We read it standing at the stove. We read it again at the counter. By the time we sat down to eat we had constructed a small theatrical production in our head where the coach was dismissive, vaguely contemptuous, and possibly planning to bench our kid this Saturday out of spite. We showed it to our spouse. Our spouse, who was eating, said *that seems fine?* and went back to the salad. We were not eating.

We have written some version of that email at least eight times across two kids and four sports. We have received some version of that reply almost every time. And we have spent hours of our adult life reading coded meaning into a coach's punctuation that we are now embarrassed about.

So this is the piece we wish someone had handed us in October of that year, before dinner.

## The reply means what it says

The coach got our email between practice and dinner, on a Wednesday, with 22 parents on his team chat, four other teams that he coaches in some form, a job that pays him, and his own kids. He read what we wrote. He noted what we asked. He typed five words on his phone with one hand because the other hand was opening a refrigerator.

*Thanks, will keep an eye on it* is exactly what he said. We are the ones who heard *you are paranoid and your kid will sit on the bench forever.* We invented that. He didn't say that.

The shape of the reply isn't an emotion. It's a Wednesday at 6:23pm.

## The pattern we eventually noticed

Long replies don't mean a coach cares more. Short replies don't mean a coach cares less.

We have learned this slowly, by being on the other side of it. We have written long emails when we were new to a thing and didn't know what to leave out. The good coaches in our life — the ones who have been doing this for a decade — almost always reply in three to ten words. They are not being short with us. They are being economical with the seven minutes they have between dropping off their own kid and leaving for our kid's practice.

Reading length as care is a parent mistake. We've made it. We've also spent two evenings being upset about emails that turned out to be fine.

## What we don't do anymore

We don't write the follow-up clarifying email. The one that says *I just want to make sure I was clear, what I meant was…* We've sent that follow-up. The coach has read it. The coach has been more annoyed by the follow-up than he was by the original email. He has given us less of his attention going forward, not more.

We don't text our spouse a punctuation analysis. We don't send the screenshot to a friend. We don't bring it up to the coach in person at Thursday's practice. The conversation closed when he replied. Re-opening it makes it about us instead of about our kid.

We also don't reply with a thank-you note ourselves. He doesn't need it. He doesn't have time to read it. The email is over.

## What we do instead

We watch the next two practices and the next game. We look for whether the thing we asked about actually changes. Usually it does, in some small way that wasn't going to be in an email and was always going to be visible only on the field.

If after two practices and a game we still see nothing, we send one short follow-up. *Hi Coach, just circling back on the email from two weeks ago. Have you noticed anything?* That's the entire follow-up. Specific. Brief. Not accusatory. Most of the time, that one gets a real conversation, in person, after Tuesday's practice, in the parking lot, with the cones still in the car.

That is when we get the answer we were actually looking for. Not on Wednesday at 6:23pm.

## The longer arc

The parents who burn out on coach communication are the parents who keep reading tone where there's no tone. They send a follow-up to the follow-up. They escalate to the team manager. They post in the team chat. By March, they're the parent the other parents avoid making eye contact with at the field, and the coach is screening their messages.

The parents who last are the ones who eventually figure out that *thanks, will keep an eye on it* is a reply you can actually take. Not because the coach is being warm. Because the coach is being a person on a Wednesday with a refrigerator in his other hand.

We are still, some weeks, the wrong kind of parent on this. We send a thing at 9pm that we shouldn't have sent. We re-read a reply too many times. We invent a problem that is not there.

But more weeks now, when a five-word reply lands in our inbox, we read it once. We say *good, he saw it.* We close the laptop. We go finish dinner.

The win is in the not-spiraling. The kid never knew anything happened. The coach never knew anything almost happened. The week kept going.

That is what we wanted from the email in the first place.
