Let's get this out of the way: hockey is expensive. There is no cheap way to outfit a hockey player. Even the minimum starter kit at age 7 assumes you buy half of it used.

Compared to the other sports on this site, hockey is in a category of its own. A fully outfitted 7-year-old costs roughly three to five times what a baseball or soccer player costs to gear up for the first season. It climbs from there. Travel hockey is one of the most expensive youth sports commitments in North America — ice time, tournament travel, and coaching fees add up fast.

That said, there is a clear order of priority. Skates first. Helmet second. Then stick. Then everything else. Cut corners on the gear list, never on the safety equipment.

## Ages 5–7 (Learn to skate + intro hockey)

Most rinks run a learn-to-skate program before kids start playing organized hockey. This is the right path. Kids who cannot skate cannot play hockey, and skating is harder than it looks from the stands.

Start with the learn-to-skate program. Most programs provide stick and puck time at the end. Your kid will know within a few sessions whether they want to keep going. That information is worth more than a full gear purchase.

**Skates (start with rentals or used)**

Learn-to-skate programs usually rent skates by the session. Use that until your kid is committed. Buying skates for a kid who might switch to figure skating or not come back is a fast way to spend a lot of money on gear that sits in a closet.

If you're buying: youth recreational skates are fine at this age. Don't buy high-end skates for a 5-year-old who will outgrow them in four months.

How to choose: skate fit is professional territory. Have a hockey shop fit them. Hockey skates run 1 to 1.5 sizes smaller than street shoe size. The heel should sit firmly in the heel pocket. Get them baked at the pro shop to mold the boot to your kid's foot — most shops do this free with purchase and it makes a real difference in how the skate feels.

Note: we don't link to skates on Amazon because skate fit requires a trained eye and in-person baking. A hockey shop fitting is not optional. It's the difference between a kid who loves skating and a kid who hates it because their feet hurt every session.

**A youth hockey helmet with full cage**

Required at every age in every youth league. The cage protects against pucks and sticks. Your kid will not love it. They will get used to it.

The helmet is the one piece of hockey gear you always buy new. Not because used helmets can't work, but because you cannot verify the certification date and structural integrity of a used helmet by looking at it. A helmet that took a hard impact can fail on the next one with no visible sign of damage.

How to choose: look for the HECC sticker inside the helmet. HECC certification is the standard that youth leagues require. The helmet should sit level on the head — not tipped back, not pushed forward. The chin strap should be snug, and the ear loops should align with the ears. If the helmet shifts when your kid shakes their head, it is too big.

**A starter stick**

Junior or youth flex sticks for this age. Wood is cheaper and totally fine at 5 to 7. Composite sticks are nicer but break easily, and a 6-year-old won't notice the difference in feel.

How to choose: stick length, when the kid is in skates, should reach about chin height. Slightly higher for wingers, slightly lower for defensemen. For learn-to-skate, just get a size that fits and don't overthink it.

**Pads (the full kit)**

This is where it adds up. The full pad kit for a 5 to 7-year-old:

- Shoulder pads
- Elbow pads
- Hockey pants (girdle or breezers)
- Shin guards
- Hockey gloves

Most beginning programs don't require the full kit at first. Check with the program before buying. For pure learn-to-skate, you can often get away with just the helmet and gloves for the first few sessions.

Buy pads used. A used set of shoulder pads from a kid who grew out of them is functionally the same as new. The helmet is the one exception.

**Mouthguard**

Required. A boil-and-bite mouthguard works fine.

## Ages 8–10 (House league / Mites and Squirts)

Real games begin. Body checking is not yet legal in most leagues at this level. If your kid is still going strong at 8, the gear gets a real upgrade. They are on the ice multiple times a week and the starter gear is not keeping up.

**Better skates (mid-range)**

By age 8, your kid is skating multiple times a week. Cheap skates won't last and won't perform. Mid-range skates are the right call here. Not entry-level, not top-shelf.

How to choose: get them professionally baked at a hockey shop. The boot is heated and molded to your kid's foot. Most shops do this free with purchase. It makes a real difference in fit and cuts down on the blister problem that sends kids crying off the ice in week one.

Skate sizing note: hockey skates run 1 to 1.5 sizes smaller than street shoe. A kid in a size 4 shoe is typically in a size 2.5 or 3 skate. Do not buy skates online and guess the size. Get fitted at a shop.

**A real youth helmet with cage or full shield**

Cages are standard and cheaper. Full polycarbonate shields are an option but tend to fog. Either works at this age. The fit check is the same as at 5 to 7: level on the head, chin strap snug, no movement when the head shakes.

Check the HECC expiration date inside the helmet. Helmets have a certification window, usually around six years from manufacture. A helmet you bought when your kid was 5 may be coming up on its date.

**A composite stick with the right flex**

Composite sticks have better feel and more flex options than wood. This is when the upgrade matters.

How to choose: stick flex should be roughly half of the player's body weight in pounds. A 60-pound kid wants approximately a 30-flex stick. A stick that's too stiff transfers no energy on a shot. Stick length, in skates, should reach approximately chin height.

**Position-specific gloves**

Skater gloves are standard. Goalie gloves are entirely separate equipment in a different category.

**Better pads (whole kit upgrade)**

Replace the starter shoulder pads, elbow pads, shin guards, and pants with mid-range gear. The used-gear strategy still works well here. Kids grow fast and outgrown pads rotate constantly at most rinks.

## Ages 11–12 (Travel and AAA consideration, body checking begins)

In many leagues, body checking begins at Pee Wee (11 to 12). The pads need to step up. This is also the age where families decide whether they're going house league or travel. That decision has a bigger financial impact than any gear purchase.

**High-end skates**

By Pee Wee age, foot growth slows down. Skates last longer. It is worth buying real skates. The brands fit differently — CCM, Bauer, True, and Graf all have distinct heel pocket and toe box shapes. Your kid's foot shape should drive the brand, not the price point or the logo.

How to choose: try multiple brands before committing. The heel should sit locked in the pocket without lifting when the toe points. The longest toe should barely brush the front when standing. In skating position (knees bent), the toe should pull back from the front. Side-to-side wobble means the boot is too soft or too big.

**A high-end helmet**

For body-checking ages, helmet quality matters more than at any other age. Look for helmets with a VTI 5-star safety rating if available. HECC certification is the floor, not the ceiling.

Do not buy a used helmet for a kid in a body-checking league unless you can verify the certification date and document that it has never taken a major impact.

**A serious composite stick**

Stick technology actually matters at this age. Curve, kick point, flex — your kid will have preferences by now. Talk to their coach before buying. A stick that fits one position and playing style does not fit another.

**Full pad kit with body-checking-rated gear**

Shoulder pads, elbow pads, pants and girdle, shin guards. Look for labels that say "Pee Wee" or indicate body-check rating. A pad set bought for no-contact Squirts may not have enough coverage for Pee Wee contact.

**A custom-fit mouthguard (worth the upgrade)**

For body-checking ages, a custom dental mouthguard is a real upgrade. Better protection, easier breathing, more comfortable to wear. Available through most dentists for around $50 and noticeably different from a boil-and-bite.

## Ages 13–14 (Bantam) and 15+ (Midget / High school)

The pads and skates last longer at this age — feet are approaching full size and the rapid growth phase is slowing. The cost shifts from gear to ice time, travel, and tournament fees.

A typical Bantam-level kit:

- Skates
- Helmet
- Sticks (expect to buy 2 to 4 per season — they break)
- Full pads
- Bag

**Travel hockey adds:**

- League fees
- Tournament fees, 4 to 8 tournaments per season
- Travel and hotels
- Private skill coaching

At this level, gear is the cheap part. Travel is where the real money goes. Families new to travel hockey are often surprised to find the gear budget is a fraction of the annual commitment. Go in with eyes open.

## How to choose a hockey skate (the universal test)

Three checks, and these apply at every age.

One. The heel pocket. The heel should fit firmly in the back of the boot. If the heel lifts when your kid points their toe, the boot is too big.

Two. The toe box. The longest toe should just barely brush the front when standing. When your kid bends their knees forward in skating position, the toe should pull back from the front.

Three. The ankle support. With the laces tightened to playing tightness, the ankle should be locked in. Side-to-side wobble means the boot is too big or too soft for the kid's weight.

Get them baked at the pro shop. The bake process heats the boot material to the point where it molds to the foot's unique shape. It takes about 15 minutes and is worth every minute.

## Hockey stick flex and length guide

Flex is the stiffness of the shaft. It is measured as the force (in pounds) required to flex the stick one inch.

A general starting point: flex should be approximately half of the player's body weight in pounds.

- 40 lb player → 20 flex
- 60 lb player → 30 flex
- 80 lb player → 40 flex
- 100 lb player → 50 flex

A stick that's too stiff won't load on a shot and the puck goes nowhere. A stick that's too flexible is hard to control on passes and releases poorly. Most youth sticks in the 30 to 50 flex range work fine for recreational players.

Length: in skates, the stick should reach approximately chin height for a forward or defenseman. Goalies use shorter, wider paddle sticks that are a separate category.

## Hockey helmet certification: what the HECC sticker means

HECC stands for Hockey Equipment Certification Council. The HECC sticker inside a hockey helmet means the helmet has passed independent certification testing for youth hockey. USA Hockey and most youth leagues require it.

Helmets have a certification window — typically six years from the date of manufacture, not the date of purchase. The sticker inside shows the manufacturing date. A helmet made in 2019 may be approaching the end of its certification window regardless of how much it has been used.

This is why you buy helmets new and don't use grandpa's old helmet from the garage. The sticker matters. The certification date matters. A helmet that looks fine may be out of certification or may have absorbed impacts that compromised the foam.

## The honest cost of hockey vs. other sports

No other sport on this site comes close to hockey in total annual cost. Some rough comparisons at the recreational level:

- Soccer first season: under $100 in gear
- Baseball first season: $75 to $150 in gear
- Basketball first season: under $75 in gear
- Hockey first season: $300 to $600+ in gear (even buying used)

Travel hockey at any level adds tournament fees, ice fees, and travel costs that can reach $3,000 to $8,000 per year for a single player. That is before private coaching.

The gear is not the expensive part. The ice time is.

If you're considering hockey for the first time, try the learn-to-skate program before any major purchase. Most rinks offer a multi-week intro program at a manageable cost. If your kid loves it after six weeks, the gear investment is worth making. If not, you spent a few hundred dollars on a great motor skill.

## A few honest notes on the cost

Used gear is fine for everything except the helmet. Pads, skates (with proper fit), pants, gloves, sticks — all work great used. Local rink pro shops often have used gear racks. SidelineSwap and Play It Again Sports have large inventories.

The helmet is the one piece you do not buy used unless you can verify the certification date and confirm it has never taken a major impact. Helmets degrade even without visible damage.

Equipment swaps are common in hockey communities. Most rinks have semi-annual gear swaps where outgrown equipment changes hands. Get on the email list for your rink.

Hockey families share. Older parents pass outgrown gear to younger families all the time. Ask around at the rink before you buy anything at retail. In most hockey communities, someone has the size you need and will hand it off for a fraction of what you'd pay new.
