Rugby is growing fast in American youth sports. The gear list is shorter than tackle football and longer than soccer. Most clubs provide jerseys and practice balls. You provide everything else.

## What the club always provides

Jersey (both practice and match), team socks in most programs, match balls. Confirm with your specific club, but this is the standard.

What you're always on your own for: mouthguard, cleats, and any protective gear your kid wants to wear.

## The required list

### Mouthguard

Required. Full stop. Rugby is a contact sport and the mouthguard is the single most important piece of protection in the kit.

Boil-and-bite works fine through age 12 or so. A custom dental-fit mouthguard is worth considering for older players who are competing regularly. It stays in better and is more comfortable, which means kids actually wear it.

### Rugby cleats

Soccer cleats work at younger ages. Rugby-specific cleats have a different stud pattern designed for the scrum and physical contact that soccer cleats aren't built for. By the time a kid is in U12 or U14 competitive play, proper rugby cleats matter.

What to look for: molded rubber studs (metal studs are not legal in youth play), mid to high cut for ankle support in contact situations, a tight fit that doesn't move on the foot.

## The recommended list

### Scrum cap

Not required at most youth levels but widely worn. A scrum cap protects the ears and head from abrasion during scrums and tackles. It does not prevent concussions and is not a substitute for proper tackling technique.

Most youth rugby parents buy a scrum cap by mid-season once they've watched their first few matches. Buy it early or buy it after the first game.

### Shoulder pads

Rugby shoulder pads are not the same as football shoulder pads. They're thin, lightweight pads that cover the shoulder joint and collarbone without restricting movement. In contact rugby (not touch or tag), they're worth having.

IRB and World Rugby have padding standards. Look for "IRB approved" or "World Rugby approved" on the packaging. Non-approved pads are usually not legal for competitive play.

## Tag and touch rugby (non-contact)

Tag rugby and touch rugby are the youth entry points. No contact means the gear list shrinks dramatically.

What you need for tag or touch: athletic clothes, cleats or athletic shoes, a water bottle. That's it. Most tag programs provide the flags. Show up and play.

## The real cost

Rugby is one of the cheaper contact sports to start. The entry cost is lower than tackle football by a significant margin. The ongoing cost is mostly replacement gear as kids grow.

The expensive years are competitive travel rugby, where tournament fees and travel costs are the real number. The gear itself stays manageable.
