Most sun damage that becomes melanoma later in life happens before age 18. The American Academy of Dermatology and the Skin Cancer Foundation both cite this, and both treat youth-athlete sun exposure as a population-level public-health problem.

Outdoor youth sports are the most concentrated childhood UV exposure most kids will get. The protocol that reduces the lifetime risk is plain.

**The SPF that works.** Broad-spectrum sunscreen, SPF 30 minimum, water-resistant. SPF 50 if the kid is fair-skinned, has a history of burns, or is in the water for hours. SPF 100 is largely marketing past 50.

Mineral sunscreens (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) work on contact. Chemical sunscreens need 15 to 30 minutes to absorb. Apply before practice, not at it.

**The two-finger rule.** The American Academy of Dermatology recommends about two finger-lengths of sunscreen for a kid's face and neck. About a shot-glass worth for the entire body. Most parents under-apply by half. The advertised SPF is achieved only at the recommended dose; less product means less protection.

**Reapplication, and why people get this wrong.** Reapply every two hours. Reapply immediately after swimming, sweating heavily, or toweling off, regardless of what the bottle says about water resistance. Water-resistant means 40 minutes; very water-resistant means 80 minutes. Both terms have FDA definitions.

The morning application that is supposed to last all day does not exist.

**The hat is still the best protection.** A wide-brimmed hat, a long-sleeved sun shirt, and shaded shelter at the field all do more than any sunscreen. UPF (ultraviolet protection factor) ratings on shirts are real and stable. UPF 50+ shirts block about 98 percent of UV.

For sports where head protection is required (helmets, batting helmets), the hat goes on between innings or between drills.

**Sunglasses.** UVA and UVB rays damage the eye. Look for lenses labeled "100% UV protection" or "UV400." Polycarbonate impact-resistant lenses for any sport involving balls. Ask the optometrist about youth-sport-specific lenses for the kid who plays tennis, golf, or baseball at high volume.

**The team can do this better than the family.** Programs that have a sunscreen station at registration, a shaded sideline, and a written break-time reapplication protocol see better adherence than parents trying to manage it solo. Ask your team manager to add a sunscreen pump bottle to the equipment bag. Cheaper than a single dermatology visit later.

**The melanoma math, briefly.** Skin cancer is the most common cancer diagnosed in the U.S. Melanoma is the most serious form. Most cases trace cumulatively to childhood and adolescent exposure. The point of sunscreen at age 9 is not the burn that day. It is the math at age 49.

**For coaches.** A pre-practice 30-second sunscreen check works. Not optional, not embarrassing. The team that gets used to it is the team where the parents thank you in five years.

**Specific items worth carrying.** A pump bottle of broad-spectrum SPF 30 in the equipment bag. Single-use sunscreen sticks for the face and ears. UPF 50+ long-sleeved shirts for outdoor practices. A wide-brim sideline hat for the kid not in the helmet rotation. Polarized UV400 sunglasses for any sport where the sky is in the kid's eye line.

The kid who was sun-protected in their youth-sports years has a different lifetime cancer profile than the kid who was not. It is a small lift on the calendar with a real return.
