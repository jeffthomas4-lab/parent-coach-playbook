Volleyball gear is minimal compared to most team sports but specific. The protective equipment that matters is well-defined.

**Knee pads.**

Standard in volleyball at most competitive levels. Protect against floor contact during defensive dives, rolls, and digging plays. Most players wear them; some choose not to.

The cost-benefit math:

Knee pads reduce knee contusions, abrasions, and bursitis from repetitive floor contact.

Knee pads do not prevent ACL or meniscus injuries (those are non-contact mechanisms).

Properly fit knee pads do not restrict performance.

For competitive volleyball at the club and high school levels, knee pads are standard. For recreational volleyball, they remain optional.

**Ankle braces.**

Lace-up ankle braces (semi-rigid) reduce ankle sprain recurrence in players with prior history by around 50 percent. The published evidence supports use for kids returning from sprain.

For kids without prior ankle injury, prophylactic ankle brace use is not strongly supported. The neuromuscular warm-up work matters more.

For the net-jump injury mechanism (landing on another player's foot), no equipment fully prevents the injury. The centerline-foot positioning rule is the published prevention.

**Finger taping.**

Buddy-taping previously-sprained fingers for return-to-play is reasonable. Pre-emptive taping of healthy fingers is not strongly evidence-supported.

For blockers and setters in particular, finger injuries are common. Awareness of jam-finger versus growth-plate-fracture distinction matters; the hand-finger-fracture-triage piece covers this.

**Wrist supports.**

Generally not standard in volleyball. Some players with prior wrist injuries use them.

**Shoes.**

Volleyball-specific indoor court shoes provide:

Better traction for lateral movement.

Some cushioning for jump landings.

Lower-profile uppers that allow ankle articulation.

Court shoes vs running shoes: the running shoe is too soft and unstable for the lateral demands of volleyball. The cross-training shoe is acceptable; the dedicated court shoe is better.

**Compression sleeves.**

Marketed for volleyball. The compression-sleeves-and-athletic-tape piece covers the framework. Modest benefit, mostly placebo. Not necessary but not harmful.

**Eyewear.**

Polycarbonate impact-resistant lenses for kids who wear glasses. Standard sport-eyewear principles.

**The hidden gear: the spike-approach training.**

The single best safety equipment for volleyball is not equipment. It is good training in approach footwork and landing mechanics. Programs that emphasize technique produce kids who jump and land safely; programs that emphasize hitting power without technique produce knee and ankle injuries.

The neuromuscular warm-up programs (FIFA 11+ and volleyball-adapted equivalents) reduce ankle and knee injury rates. Most volleyball programs do not run them.

**For coaches.**

Knee pads are standard. Verify players have them.

Ankle bracing for returning-from-sprain players.

Training emphasis on landing mechanics.

Pre-practice neuromuscular warm-up.

**For families.**

The gear list for volleyball is short and inexpensive relative to other sports. The high-leverage items are court shoes (good ones) and knee pads (standard).

**The honest read.** Volleyball gear protects against the predictable but does not change the underlying injury patterns. The neuromuscular warm-up and the technique work matter more than the equipment. For kids in competitive programs, the standard gear plus the published prevention protocols produce safer athletes.
