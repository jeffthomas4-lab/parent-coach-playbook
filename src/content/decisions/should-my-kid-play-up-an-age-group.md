title: "Should My Kid Play Up an Age Group?"
summary: "Playing up can accelerate development or grind a kid down. Here's how to tell which one you're dealing with."
theQuestion: "My kid is being recruited to play up. Should we say yes?"
benefits:
  - "Faster development from facing tougher competition."
  - "May keep a physically or mentally advanced kid more engaged."
  - "Can build confidence if the kid handles it well."
  - "Opens doors to better coaches and higher-level programs."
costs:
  - "Physical mismatch at younger ages can lead to injury."
  - "A kid who dominates their age group but struggles playing up can take a real confidence hit."
  - "Social dynamics are harder when you're the youngest on the team."
  - "The pressure to justify the bump-up can wear on a kid fast."
signsItsAGoodFit:
  - "The kid is physically mature for their age, not just skilled."
  - "The kid is asking for a bigger challenge. Not the parents."
  - "The new level is one year up, not two or three."
  - "The coach in the higher group is expecting the kid to develop, not perform immediately."
signsItsNot:
  - "The push is coming from the parent or the club, not the kid."
  - "The physical gap at the new level is significant."
  - "The kid is already showing stress or anxiety about their current level."
  - "The kid's current team would lose a key player and the social cost matters to your kid."
howToHandleIt:
  - "Ask the kid directly if they want the challenge. Not in front of the coach."
  - "Watch one practice or game at the higher level before committing."
  - "Agree on a trial period, four to six weeks, before making it permanent."
  - "If the kid struggles socially more than athletically, that's data worth weighing."
theRule: "Playing up should serve the kid's development, not the club's roster needs."
