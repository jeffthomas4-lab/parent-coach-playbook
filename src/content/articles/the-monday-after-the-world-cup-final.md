Monday morning, before we'd even finished a cup of coffee, our kid was already in the backyard kicking a ball against the fence. The final had ended the evening before, the trophy had been lifted, the tournament that had taken over our television and our kid's imagination for two and a half weeks was officially over. And yet here they were at 7am, still completely full of it, with nowhere obvious to put all that energy now that there wasn't another match to watch that night.

We recognized the feeling before our kid could have named it. It's the same letdown that follows any big, immersive thing once it ends, a concert, a family trip, a season finale. The World Cup had given our kid a month of daily excitement, something to look forward to every evening, players to admire, moments to talk about at breakfast. Monday took all of that away at once, and what was left was a kid standing in the backyard at dawn with a ball and no tournament to watch that night.

**Our first instinct was to solve it with a commitment, and we caught ourselves before we did.** It would have been easy to use the momentum of the moment to sign our kid up for something big: a club team, a fall league registration, a specialized camp. The excitement was right there, practically begging to be channeled into something official. But a kid's enthusiasm on the Monday after a month-long tournament is not the same thing as a kid's settled, tested interest in playing more soccer over the long haul. One is an afterglow. The other takes weeks to actually show itself, and the two can look identical on a Monday morning.

**We looked for small outlets instead of big ones.** The backyard kickabout our kid had already started on their own was actually the right instinct, just informal enough to burn off the energy without committing anyone to anything. We leaned into that rather than redirecting it. A few evenings that week, we kicked the ball around after dinner. We texted a friend's parent to see if their kids wanted to meet at the park for a pickup game, no coach, no league, no fee, just a ball and some space. None of it required registration forms or a new line item in the family budget.

**We let our kid keep watching soccer on their own terms, even with the tournament over.** There's other soccer to watch even after a World Cup final, domestic leagues, replays of tournament highlights, and our kid spent part of that week doing exactly that, watching clips of their favorite moments again, still processing the month that had just happened. We didn't push this along or discourage it. A kid working through the end of something they loved by revisiting it for a few more days isn't stuck. They're just not ready to let go yet, and that's fine.

**We didn't treat the leftover energy as a decision point that needed resolving immediately.** There was no reason to have a serious conversation that week about whether our kid wanted to play club soccer, switch from their current sport, or add anything new to the fall calendar. Those are real questions, and they deserve real answers, but they don't need those answers on the Monday after a tournament ends, when the excitement is at its most inflated and the kid hasn't had any time to see what's left once the daily matches stop.

**We noticed the energy showed up in unexpected places too, not just the backyard.** Our kid started drawing soccer fields in the margins of a summer workbook, narrating imaginary matches to a younger sibling, asking to wear a soccer shirt to a cousin's birthday party that had nothing to do with sports. None of that needed managing. It was just what a month of total immersion in a tournament looks like once it spills out sideways for a week or two after the thing itself has ended. We let it spill.

**We also watched for the difference between excitement and restlessness.** By midweek, some of what looked like leftover World Cup energy was actually our kid just being bored, the ordinary kind of summer boredom that has nothing to do with soccer specifically. We didn't assume every request to "do something soccer" was really about soccer. Sometimes it was, and we said yes. Sometimes a walk to the park or a board game solved the same restlessness just as well, and we didn't force a soccer frame onto a moment that didn't need one.
