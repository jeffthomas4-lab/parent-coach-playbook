Ask a track parent if their kid made the team and they'll look at you funny. Almost everyone makes the team. The real question is which meets your kid actually competes in, and that's decided inside a group of four or five teammates, not against the whole roster.

**The team is big. The event group is small.** A program might carry 80 or 100 kids, but your kid's actual competition is the handful of teammates running the same race or throwing the same implement. Most schools have somewhere around four to six kids who can compete for a serious spot in any one event, and dual meets usually allow two or three entries per school per individual event. That's the real roster your kid is fighting.

**A scoring spot means beating your own teammates first.** Meets only score the athletes a school actually enters, so if five teammates run the 400 and the coach can only enter three, two of them don't score no matter how their time would rank statewide. The conversation your kid needs isn't "am I fast," it's "am I one of my school's top three in this event right now."

**Relay legs run on a slightly wider pool, and that's the real opportunity.** A 4x100 or 4x400 usually pulls from five or six candidates for four spots, sometimes more if a coach mixes and matches across events. A kid who isn't quite fast enough to score individually can find a relay leg instead, especially in the 400 and 800 where endurance and speed both matter. Ask directly whether relay legs are open to more than the top four in an event.

**Varsity letters usually run on their own separate rule, not raw talent.** Most programs award letters off a combination of participation, meets competed in, and a scoring or time standard set by the coaching staff, not a subjective ranking. A distance kid who never wins a race but hits every practice and clears the standard letters. Ask your coach for the specific letter policy in writing; it varies by school and it's rarely announced until someone asks.

**Within an event group, the stratification is real even though the roster spot isn't in question.** A throws group might have one kid who's clearly the school's top thrower, two more fighting for the second scoring spot, and three more who compete mostly at dual meets and rarely at invitationals. Nobody got cut. Everybody still knows exactly where they stand, because the results post publicly after every meet.

**What actually moves a kid up the group.** Consistency beats one big meet, since coaches enter based on a season's pattern, not a single outlier performance. A kid who PRs at the right meet, usually a bigger invitational with faster competition pushing the pace, tends to move up faster than one who only PRs at small dual meets. The [track and field pathway](/pathways/track-field/) covers how event specialization develops through the teenage years, which is useful context for why some event groups are deeper at some schools than others.

Track never sends your kid home. It just tells them, in public, exactly where the line is inside their own event group. That's a different kind of pressure than a cut, and it deserves a different kind of conversation in the car.
