I yelled at a twelve-year-old umpire last spring. Not at my kid. Not at the other team. At a kid three years older than my own son who was working the plate alone because the league couldn't find a second official.

He missed a call. I stood up out of the dugout and said something I don't repeat here. My son was on deck. He heard it.

That's the moment I mean when I say the hardest player on my roster is me.

## The trigger isn't the thing you think it is

I used to believe I lost it over bad calls. I don't. I lose it when my own kid is struggling and I feel like I can't fix it from the dugout.

The ref is just the closest thing to yell at. He's not the reason.

Watch yourself for a season and you'll find the same pattern. The blowup rarely lines up with the actual bad call. It lines up with the inning your kid struck out looking, or the quarter your kid rode the bench, or the moment you clocked the other parent's kid outplaying yours. The trigger is your own kid's performance wearing a referee costume.

## What it costs the team

The team is watching you before they're watching the game. Nine kids see the coach lose his composure over an umpire's strike zone. They learn that composure is optional when the stakes feel personal.

Your own kid sees something worse. They see that their bad at-bat made you erupt at somebody else. That's a heavier thing to carry to second base than a strikeout is.

Assistant coaches stop trusting you with close games. Not because you're wrong about the call. Because you're unpredictable, and unpredictable coaches make everyone on the bench tighten up.

## The reset I actually use

Between innings, I count sets of four. Four seconds in, hold, four seconds out. Not because it's magic. Because it gives my hands something to do besides pointing at an official.

Before I say anything to a player after a mistake, I wait for the walk from the mound to the dugout. Thirty feet. That's the buffer. If I still want to say the sharp thing after thirty feet, I say a softer version of it. Usually I don't want to say it at all by then.

I stopped standing at the fence closest to home plate. I moved to the far end of the dugout. Distance from the strike zone is distance from the trigger.

And I built one rule that isn't about technique: I don't correct my own kid until we're in the car, and even then only if they ask. The dugout correction was never for them. It was for me, so the other parents could see I wasn't playing favorites. That's my problem to manage, not a lesson to deliver mid-game.

## The part I don't love admitting

Some of this isn't really about coaching. It's about watching my own kid struggle in public and not being able to protect them from it, the way I could when they were four and I could just pick them up.

I can't pick them up anymore. I can only manage how I look while I watch.

That's a loss, and I don't think it gets talked about enough in the coaching books. The parent-coach isn't just managing a roster. He's grieving a version of parenting where he could fix things by picking the kid up.

## What changed when I fixed this

The umpire thing hasn't happened again. Not because I got better at reading blown calls. Because I stopped needing an outlet for something that was never about the call.

My son stopped watching me between plays. He used to check my face after every pitch. Now he mostly doesn't, which means he's playing the game instead of managing my reaction to it.

The team plays looser in close innings. Kids copy the adult in charge. When I stopped needing a target for my own frustration, they stopped needing one either.

I still get frustrated. I coach because I care, and caring produces heat. The difference is where the heat goes. It used to go at a twelve-year-old with a chest protector. Now it goes into four counts of breath and thirty feet of walking room.

That's the whole fix. Not less feeling. A shorter fuse doesn't get replaced with no fuse. It gets replaced with a longer one.
