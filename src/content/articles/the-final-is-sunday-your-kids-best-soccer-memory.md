The final kicks off Sunday at 3pm ET at MetLife Stadium in East Rutherford, and our kid has been counting down to it since the semifinals ended. We started thinking, a few days out, about what would actually make the afternoon memorable for a kid who plays this sport every week, rather than just another Sunday where the television happened to be on longer than usual.

Our first instinct was to plan something big. A themed spread, a jersey for every family member, maybe invite the whole team over. We talked ourselves out of most of that once we thought about what our kid actually remembers from past big sports days. It's never the decorations. It's small, specific moments: someone explaining a play at exactly the right time, a snack they got to pick themselves, a piece of the day where they were doing something instead of just watching.

**We're keeping the guest list small.** A house full of people during a final can turn into background noise, adults talking through the match, kids losing interest halfway through because there's too much else going on. We're keeping it to our own family and maybe one other family whose kid plays with ours, small enough that our kid can actually talk through the match with us instead of getting lost in a crowd. A big watch party might be exactly right for some families. Ours does better with fewer people and more actual attention on the game.

**We're letting our kid pick the food, within reason.** Not a themed menu built around whichever countries are playing. Just their favorite snack, the same one they'd want on any other special day. The point isn't to build a soccer aesthetic around the afternoon. It's to make the afternoon feel good in whatever way already means something to our specific kid, which in our house is a very specific kind of snack mix that has nothing to do with soccer at all.

**We're playing some soccer before kickoff.** Fifteen minutes in the backyard or the driveway, nothing structured, just kicking a ball around together before the match starts. This is the piece we think matters most. A kid who plays the sport connects to a televised final differently if they've just had their own hands, or feet, on a ball that same day. It turns the final from something happening to other people into something that rhymes with what our kid does every Tuesday at practice.

**We're planning to talk during the match, not just watch it silently.** Not a running commentary that talks over the actual game, but small, well-timed observations: why a team is holding possession in the midfield, what a substitution might be signaling, why a particular foul earned a card and another one didn't. Our kid picks up more from a few well-placed comments during a match than from any pregame explainer we could give them beforehand. The trick is timing it to moments that matter and staying quiet the rest of the time, since constant narration gets tuned out fast.

**We're not making a big deal out of who wins.** Kids notice when a parent has an emotional stake in a result that has nothing to do with their own life, and reacting too big to either team's win or loss risks making the afternoon about the score instead of about the experience of watching a great match together. We're planning to be excited about good plays regardless of which team makes them, and to let our kid have whatever preference they've picked up over the tournament without either of us performing a bigger reaction than we actually feel.

**We're leaving room for our kid to process it afterward, on their own terms.** Some kids want to talk through a final in detail once it's over, replaying moments, asking questions about specific decisions. Others are ready to move on to something else within minutes. We're not planning a structured debrief. We're just staying available if our kid wants to talk about it over dinner, and not pushing if they don't.

**We thought about whether to invite our kid's own coach or teammates, and decided against making it that formal.** A team watch party sounds like a nice idea on paper, and for some families it probably is exactly right. For us, it felt like it would turn a relaxed Sunday into another version of a team event, with all the same social dynamics and logistics as a normal team gathering, when what we actually wanted was something smaller and quieter that centered on our own kid rather than the group. We might ask a teammate's family next time. This year we kept it simple on purpose.
