The tournament bracket went up on the league website back in May, three pool play games on Saturday, bracket play Sunday, a Friday-night arrival window because the fields are two hours from home. Nobody involved chose the 4th of July weekend specifically. It's just the weekend that was open on the fields' calendar, and it happened to also be the weekend our extended family usually gathers at a lake house that's been in the family for three decades.

We found out about the conflict in early June, which gave us time to plan around it instead of discovering it on July 3rd, but it still meant a real conversation about what we were giving up.

**The direct cost is money, and it's worth naming honestly.** Holiday-weekend hotel rates near tournament fields run higher than a normal weekend, sometimes considerably higher, because every other tournament family is booking the same three or four hotels near the complex. Gas for a two-hour drive each way, meals out for three days instead of a home cookout, a tournament entry fee that was already budgeted before the holiday collision became clear. None of this is unusual for travel tournaments generally. It's just concentrated into a weekend that would otherwise have been free.

**The less obvious cost is the missed gathering.** Our extended family's lake weekend isn't replaceable on a different date, because it only happens once a year and it only happens because everyone clears the same weekend for it. Skipping a Tuesday practice can be made up. Skipping the one weekend a year four generations of the same family are in the same place cannot. We didn't pretend that trade was even. We named it as what it was: a real cost, paid for a tournament our kid wanted to play.

**We didn't make the decision for our kid or hide it from them.** We told them directly that the tournament weekend meant missing the lake trip, and asked whether they still wanted to play. They did, and they said so clearly, which mattered to us. A kid who is old enough to understand what they're giving up and still chooses the tournament is making a real choice, not a choice we made and are attributing to them after the fact. A younger kid might not have the context to weigh that tradeoff fairly, and in that case the decision leans more on the parents' judgment about what the family can actually afford to lose that particular year.

**We also talked to the extended family directly instead of letting them find out secondhand.** A grandparent or an aunt hearing about a missed holiday gathering through a group text feels different than hearing it from us in a phone call, with context about why and an offer to make up for it another way. We called ahead of time, explained the tournament, and said we'd try to get to the lake house for a long weekend later in the summer instead. Nobody was thrilled about the timing, but nobody felt blindsided either, and that distinction tends to matter more to extended family than the missed weekend itself.

**We priced out the actual cost before committing, rather than after.** Holiday weekend hotel rates near the tournament complex ran close to forty percent higher than a normal weekend rate at the same properties, based on what we found when we compared dates a few weeks apart. Knowing that number ahead of time let us decide whether the tournament was worth it with real information, instead of registering first and discovering the price shock once we went looking for a room in late June when most of the nearby hotels were already full anyway.
