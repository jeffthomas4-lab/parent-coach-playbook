A 9-year-old goes 0 for 4 in a Saturday game. The parent-coach drives him home. Twelve minutes in the car. The conversation in those twelve minutes determines what every dinner, every breakfast, and every practice for the next seven days feels like in that house.

Most parent-coaches I know would prepare for the lineup card more carefully than they prepare for those twelve minutes.

That is the problem this publication exists to fix.

## The framework

Every game has three drives. The drive there. The game itself. The drive home.

The drive there is roughly fifteen minutes of pre-game prep. Mindset. The job for today. The reduction of stakes. The handoff at the parking lot.

The game is two hours of coaching execution. Substitutions. Adjustments. The strange and specific job of correcting your own kid in front of eleven other kids who are watching to see how you handle it.

The drive home is forty-five minutes long if you count the dinner table that night. The first ninety seconds of those forty-five minutes are the most important sentences you will speak to your kid all week.

Three drives. One relationship.

## Most coaches obsess over the middle

The middle is the part everybody talks about. The film. The play call. The pre-game speech. The lineup. The clinics, the certifications, the Twitter threads on coaching philosophy. All middle.

The middle is also the smallest part of the relationship.

Two hours on the field. Twenty-two hours in the car, the kitchen, the back yard, the bathroom doorway at eleven at night when your kid finally tells you what they were upset about. The middle is one ninth of the day. The bookends are everything else.

The bookends are where development happens. Not skill development. The other kind. The kind that decides whether your kid still wants to be doing this in three years.

## What the bookends actually do

The drive there sets the temperature for the day. If you spend it drilling the lineup or strategy, the kid hears pressure. If you spend it grounding them, reducing stakes, and handing them one specific job, they walk into the dugout calm and brave.

The drive home decides what the next week looks like in your house. The first ninety seconds are a window. The kid is asking, without using these words: *is this still a place I want to be?* The answer they get from your face and your first sentence sets the rest of the week.

This is the part nobody warned us about. There is no certification for the drive home.

## Coach the team. Keep the kid.

The hardest job in youth sports is treating your kid like every other kid for two hours, then putting them back in the car as your kid.

Most parent-coaches handle the transition wrong. They overcorrect to look fair, which the kid hears as dad is mad. Or they overcompensate to protect, which the team hears as the coach has a favorite. Either way, the kid notices. All eleven other kids notice.

The rule we landed on, after one season of getting it wrong and one season of starting to get it right: coach the team in the dugout. Keep the kid in the car. Both jobs are full-time. Neither one is more important than the other.

If you cannot do both, get a different parent to coach the team. There are seasons when that is the right call. We are saying that out loud because nobody else will.

## The eight sports and the activities that aren't sports

Baseball. Softball. Soccer. Basketball. Football. Hockey. Lacrosse. Volleyball. We cover all eight, by sport and by age band, because the right sentence for an 8-year-old soccer player is not the right sentence for a 14-year-old hockey player.

But the framework also fits the things that aren't sports. The choir concert has a drive there, a performance, and a drive home. So does opening night for a school play. So does an orchestra solo. The first ninety seconds after a 12-year-old's first violin recital are the same physics as the first ninety seconds after a baseball game. We watched two kids grow up moving through both kinds of activities. The rules don't change.

## Why this publication exists

There are two kinds of writing about youth sports right now. There is loud, primary-color stuff aimed at the wrong parent: the one yelling on the sideline. And there is anodyne, soft-focus stuff that talks about journey and growth and never says anything specific.

This is the third kind. Adult, serious, specific. Written for the parent-coach who already knows the easy version of this is wrong and wants the harder version that actually works.

We will name the sentences that work. We will name the ones that don't. We will tell you what to say in the parking lot before a 5-year-old's first tee-ball game and what to say to a 16-year-old who just got cut from varsity. We will tell you when to shut up. We will tell you which parts of this are bigger than the sport.

The drive home is the real game. Most parent-coaches are still warming up for it when it ends. That stops here.
