By the time the semifinals arrived, our kid had a favorite player. Not a favorite team, a favorite player, with a jersey number they'd started drawing in the corner of their school notebook and a habit of narrating that player's every touch out loud during matches he wasn't even in. Semifinal week, with two matches, one on July 14 and one on July 15, gave our kid two more chances to watch this player closely, and the attachment sharpened with every minute of screen time.

We didn't think much of it at first. Kids attach to athletes the way they attach to characters in a show, quickly and completely, and it usually burns off on its own schedule. What made us pay closer attention this week was a comment our eleven-year-old made at dinner, something like "he's probably the nicest guy in the world," delivered with total confidence about a professional athlete they had watched play soccer on television and knew absolutely nothing else about.

**That's the line worth noticing.** Admiring how someone plays is different from deciding who they are as a person based on a few televised appearances. Our kid wasn't wrong to admire the player's skill. The footwork was genuinely remarkable, the composure under pressure was the kind of thing worth pointing out and learning from. But "he's probably the nicest guy in the world" isn't a comment about skill. It's a guess about character, built entirely out of a highlight reel and a jersey number, and guesses like that tend to calcify into something a kid holds onto as fact.

**We asked what specifically they admired, and kept steering the conversation back to that.** When our kid brought up the player unprompted, we'd ask a followup: what did he do in that match that impressed you. Sometimes the answer was tactical, the way he found space before a pass arrived. Sometimes it was about composure, how he didn't panic under pressure late in a match. Those are real, useful things to admire, and they're things our kid can actually try to apply to their own game on a Tuesday practice field. We tried not to let the conversation drift into territory neither of us actually had any information about, like what the player is like with his own family or how he treats people off camera, because none of that is knowable from watching him play soccer for ninety minutes.

**We didn't correct the enthusiasm, we just narrowed it.** There's a version of this conversation that comes off as deflating a kid's excitement, telling them not to get so attached to someone they'll never meet. We didn't do that, and we don't think it would have worked anyway. What we did instead was gently separate the two things our kid had bundled together: the player's skill, which is real and worth studying, and the player's character, which our kid was inventing wholesale because eleven-year-olds like their heroes to be good all the way through. We said something like, "We don't actually know what he's like as a person, we've only seen him play. But that touch he had in the box was incredible, let's watch it again."

**This matters more at eleven and twelve than it did a few years earlier.** Younger kids tend to admire athletes in a looser, more cartoonish way, closer to admiring a superhero than a real person. Around eleven and twelve, kids start building more detailed mental models of the people they admire, deciding not just that a player is good at soccer but that the player represents some larger idea of what a good person or a successful person looks like. That's a normal part of growing up. It's also exactly the point where an unexamined attachment to a stranger on television can start shaping a kid's sense of what to value, without anyone checking whether the model is built on anything real.

**We also talked, once, about the difference between a player's public performance and their actual private life.** Not in a cynical way, and not to suggest anything negative about any specific player, since we don't know anything negative and wouldn't say so if we did. Just as a plain fact: everyone we watch on television, in any sport, is showing us a small, curated slice of themselves, the slice that happens during a match, and a kid who understands that early tends to hold their heroes more loosely and more fairly, admiring what's actually visible instead of inventing the rest.

None of this stopped our kid from watching the semifinals with total devotion, tracking their favorite player's every touch, or drawing that jersey number in the notebook margin again on Wednesday. We didn't want it to stop. We just wanted the admiration aimed at something real, the footwork, the calm under pressure, the decision-making in tight spaces, rather than at a full imagined personality our kid had built out of two matches and a highlight reel. Skill is something a kid can actually learn from and try to copy at their own practice. An invented character isn't, and it tends to disappoint a kid later when the real, fuller person eventually shows up in the news being complicated, like every person eventually is.

The final is still a few days away. Our kid's favorite player might be in it, might not be. Either way, we're planning to keep asking the same question when the admiration comes up: what did he actually do that impressed you. It's a small habit, but it's the one that keeps a kid's hero worship pointed at something they can use.
