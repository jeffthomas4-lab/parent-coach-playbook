The word "cheer" is doing a lot of work. Two sports use it, they share aesthetics and vocabulary, and they have almost nothing else in common. Before you go to a tryout, before you pay a gym deposit, you need to know which sport your family is entering.

Sideline cheer is what most people picture. Your kid cheers at the school's football and basketball games, does pep rallies, possibly goes to a regional competition in the spring. The season follows the school calendar. The cost for the season lands somewhere between $200 and $800 depending on the uniform situation and whether the school requires a spirit pack. It is a school activity that takes 6 to 10 hours a week during football season.

All-star cheer is a different sport. It happens at a private gym, runs year-round, competes at judged events on Saturdays from October through May, and the best teams go to Worlds at ESPN Wide World of Sports in Orlando in April or the US Finals in the spring. The routine is two and a half minutes, choreographed, performed on a spring floor, and judged on execution, tumbling difficulty, stunting, and synchronization. The cost for a typical all-star year runs $4,000 to $6,000 for a mid-level program, and you can see the full breakdown in [what competitive cheer actually costs](/drive-there/how-much-does-competitive-cheer-cost/).

All-star uses a leveling system governed by the USASF: Level 1 through Level 7, with Level 1 being the entry point and Level 7 being the top of the sport. Most kids start at Level 1. Level 1 is tumbling limited to standing back walkovers and round-offs; no back handsprings. Level 2 adds the back handspring. Level 3 adds the back tuck. The level system matters because it controls what skills your kid's team is allowed to do in competition. A gym that talks about levels casually without being able to explain where your kid would place is not a gym you want.

Before Level 2, your kid needs to have a back walkover and a clean round-off. Those are the realistic gates. A gym that accepts a brand-new tumbler into Level 2 without those skills is either going to spend the whole season trying to catch them up or putting them in a spot in the routine where they don't show their tumbling. Neither is good for the kid.

Tryout culture varies by gym. Some gyms do formal skill evaluations with a score sheet; others do a day-camp tryout where coaches watch kids learn a short routine. In both cases, show up in practice clothes, cheer shoes if you have them, hair up and out of the face, and ready to follow instructions in a group. The coaches are watching for coachability at least as much as skill. A brand-new cheerleader who picks up an eight-count quickly and hits the motions clean is more valuable to most programs than a gymnast who can't count music.

The competition schedule is worth mapping before the season starts. Most competitions are on Saturdays, October through April. Regional events are usually within driving distance. National events, including Worlds at ESPN Wide World of Sports in Kissimmee, Florida, and US Finals, involve flights and hotels and full-week commitments. Your Level 1 or Level 2 kid is almost certainly not going to Worlds in year one, but it is worth asking the gym which competitions are mandatory versus optional and what travel looks like for their program before you commit. Some gyms require attendance at every event. Some leave nationals optional. That is a meaningful difference in your calendar and your budget.

The uniform cost is the thing that surprises families most. The team uniform is custom ordered at the beginning of the season, runs $300 to $500 for the full set including bow and shoes, and cannot be sourced independently. Do not buy anything uniform-shaped before the gym tells you exactly what the team is wearing. The colors, the style, and the vendor change with every season and sometimes mid-season if the gym rebrands. The families who show up with the wrong color bow are a real phenomenon.

On gear: cheer shoes are the only specific purchase. A basic white cheer shoe from Nfinity, Varsity, or a comparable brand runs $50 to $80 and will last two to three seasons. The spring-floor cushioning and the ankle support make a real difference compared to running shoes on a spring floor. Everything else in the bag for year one is practice clothes they already own, hair gel, bobby pins, a brush, and athletic tape.

What practice actually looks like: conditioning first, then stunting with a dedicated stunt group, then tumbling work, then running the routine. All-star practices run two to three hours, two to four times a week. Kids come home tired, especially in the first month. That is not a warning sign; that is what building a base looks like.

Competition day is not a soccer Saturday. The team's performance slot is 10 to 15 minutes. The day is nine hours. Call time for hair and makeup is usually 5:30 or 6:00am. You sit in an arena with thousands of other cheer families, your kid performs for two and a half minutes, you wait for scores, and then you do it again in two or three weeks. The [competition packing list](/drive-there/cheer-competition-packing-list/) will keep you from forgetting the thing that matters most the morning of.

The car ride home from a competition is different from the car ride home from a game. The score is posted. The ranking is visible. Your kid knows exactly how they did relative to every other team in the building. The car is not the place to process the score. Ask them about a specific moment in the routine, something you saw them nail, before you ask anything about placement. That is the order that works.
