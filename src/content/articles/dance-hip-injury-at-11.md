Your 11-year-old has been complaining about her hip for a few weeks. Sometimes it pops. Sometimes it aches after class. The studio teacher said to ice it.

Here is how to think about this.

## Why dance hips are at risk

Dance asks the hip to do things normal life does not.

Turnout. The hip has to rotate outward to about 45 degrees per leg, with the goal of close to 90 degrees over years of training. This puts continuous stress on the hip's labrum, capsule, and surrounding muscles.

Extension. Lifting the leg above 90 degrees, sometimes well above, repeated thousands of times per year.

Jumps and landings. Repetitive impact on a joint that is being asked to be hypermobile.

The combination is unique. A young dancer's hip undergoes more cumulative load than the hip of a same-aged kid in most other sports.

## Common dance hip issues

A few specific things that show up.

Hip flexor strain. The muscles in the front of the hip get overworked from repeated leg lifts. Pain is in the front of the hip, sometimes radiating to the upper thigh. Usually resolves with rest and stretching.

Hip popping or snapping. Often called "snapping hip syndrome." A tendon or muscle catches on a bony prominence as it moves over it. The pop is audible. Sometimes painless. Sometimes uncomfortable. Usually not dangerous but can become inflamed with overuse.

Labral tears. The labrum is the cartilage ring around the hip socket. Tears can happen from chronic stress over time. Pain is usually deep in the hip and gets worse with activity. This is more serious and often requires imaging.

Stress fractures. The femur and pelvic bones can develop small fractures from repetitive impact. Pain is deep, persistent, and gets worse with weight-bearing. Stress fractures are easy to miss without proper imaging.

Hip impingement. The bones of the hip socket do not fit together quite right and the joint pinches with deep flexion. Pain is in the groin, especially with turnout. Can require surgery if severe.

## What to watch for

Most parents bring their kid in too late. The reason is that most hip issues hurt only intermittently at first. The kid pushes through.

Signs you should not ignore.

Pain that has been there for more than two weeks despite ice and rest.

Pain that gets worse during class, not just after.

Pain that wakes the kid up at night.

A noticeable limp on the way to the car after class.

Pain in the groin that radiates to the inner thigh.

Loss of range of motion. The kid cannot extend the leg as far as she could last month.

Clicking or popping with pain. (Painless popping is usually fine.)

Any of these is a reason to see a doctor. All of them together is a reason to stop dance until you do.

## Why the general pediatrician is not enough

A general pediatrician will examine the kid, probably tell you to rest and ice, and possibly suggest physical therapy.

This is usually fine for minor strains. It is not enough for anything structural.

The right specialist for serious dance hip issues is a sports medicine physician, ideally one who works with performing artists or dancers specifically. Many cities have dance medicine clinics affiliated with universities or hospitals. These doctors know what to look for.

A sports medicine physician will examine the hip with specific tests for impingement, labral involvement, and stress fractures. They will order imaging if appropriate. They will refer to physical therapy with a therapist who treats dancers.

If your kid has been at the pediatrician for hip pain for three weeks and it is not improving, ask the pediatrician for a referral to a sports medicine doctor. If they hesitate, find one yourself. Most reputable sports medicine clinics will take a self-referral.

## The imaging question

If the doctor orders imaging, here is what to know.

X-rays are first. They show bone problems like fractures and structural variations.

MRI shows soft tissue. The labrum, tendons, and muscles. If a labral tear is suspected, MRI is the test.

Most kids do not need imaging for the first episode of hip pain. They do need it if the pain has persisted for more than a few weeks despite proper rest.

## Rest is harder than parents realize

The hardest prescription is rest.

A dancer who is told to take six weeks off will fight you on every day of it. They will worry about losing their place in the studio. They will worry about losing technique. They will worry about being behind.

A few moves that help.

Get specific from the doctor. "Six weeks of no jumping, no extensions above 45 degrees, no turnout work" is more livable than "rest." The kid can sometimes stay in class for barre work and basic technique without aggravating the injury.

Talk to the studio. Most studios will accommodate an injured dancer. Some will let them sit through class taking notes. Some will give them physical therapy exercises to do alongside the regular dancers.

Cross-train. Swimming is excellent for hip injuries because it offloads the joint while maintaining cardiovascular conditioning. Pilates, done carefully, can maintain core strength.

Address the underlying cause. Most hip injuries are not random. They are caused by overtraining, poor technique, or growth spurts. Use the rest period to fix the cause, not just heal the injury.

## The repeat injury risk

Once a kid has had a hip injury, the chance of another one is higher.

The reasons.

Compensation patterns developed during the injury linger. The kid favors the injured side without realizing it.

The kid returns to full training too quickly because they feel better.

The underlying technique issue is not corrected.

A good return-to-dance plan is gradual. The kid does barre only for two weeks. Then half center work. Then full center but no jumps. Then jumps in small doses. The full return to performance level should take twice as long as the initial recovery period.

If the studio cannot or will not respect a gradual return, that is a serious problem. Find a studio that will.

## A note on growth and pain

Some hip pain in 11 to 14 year olds is growth-related. The bones are growing faster than the soft tissues. The hip flexors get tight, the iliac crest gets sore, the femoral epiphysis can be tender.

This pain is real. It is also usually self-limiting. The kid grows. The pain resolves over a few months.

The challenge is telling growth pain from injury pain. They feel similar. The doctor can usually tell. The non-doctor cannot.

When in doubt, the answer is rest plus imaging. Better to find out it was just growth than to miss a stress fracture.

## The long view

Most kids who have one hip injury recover and continue dancing. A few do not, but most do.

The biggest predictor of whether a kid keeps dancing after a hip injury is how the family and studio handle the injury. Patient recovery. Real imaging if needed. Honest conversations about whether the training load is sustainable.

Pushed-through injuries become career-ending. Treated injuries become temporary setbacks.

Take the hip pain seriously. The kid will thank you when she is 25.
