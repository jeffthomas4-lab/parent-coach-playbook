# Illustration prompts for Parent Coach Playbook

Use these with any AI image tool (Midjourney, DALL·E, Imagen, ChatGPT image, Adobe Firefly). Each prompt is calibrated to the brand: warm editorial, mom-friendly without being arts-and-crafty, kitchen-table real, never sports-magazine-glossy.

## The brand brief (paste this once at the top of any session)

> Editorial illustration in the style of warm, contemporary magazine work (think *The Atlantic* lifestyle vertical, *Eater*'s parenting illustration, *The Washington Post*'s weekend section). Limited palette: cream paper background (#FAF6EE), warm dark brown ink line (#2D2520), terracotta (#C5713D), sage (#8FA68C), honey (#D4AB6A), dusty rose (#B8908F). Clean linework, gentle hand-drawn quality, slight grain or paper texture. Faces ambiguous or partially obscured — never identifiable people. Composition leaves negative space on the right side or top for headline overlay. NEVER: chalkboard backgrounds, script handwritten lettering, pastel everything, neon colors, glossy sports-magazine action shots, posed family portraits, watermarks, stock-photo lighting. Aspect ratios noted per prompt. Mood: warm, lived-in, observed, kind. The viewer should feel they are remembering a moment, not watching one.

## Hero illustrations for cornerstones (3 pieces, 16:9)

### 1. "Three drives. One relationship." — `/drive-home/three-drives-one-relationship/`

> A car interior viewed from the dashboard, looking back. A child sized roughly six years old sits buckled in a booster seat in the back, baseball cap slightly askew, head turned to look out the side window. A parent's hand rests lightly on the back of the passenger seat — visible only as a forearm and hand. The mood is post-game, late afternoon light slanting through the rear window. A tee-ball helmet rests on the seat next to the child. Cream paper background through the windshield in front, blurred. Dusty rose and honey light. The emotional register: somebody about to find the right thing to say.

Aspect: 16:9. Use as the article hero image. Place headline over the cream/blurred space at upper left.

### 2. "Why we exist. Three things youth sports is for." — `/game/the-real-job-of-youth-sports/`

> A six-year-old in a tee-ball uniform mid-stride toward first base, captured from a low side angle. The kid is half-running, half-skipping, completely unconcerned with technique. A single blurred adult coach figure in the soft background giving a thumbs-up. The diamond is rendered loose — chalk lines suggested rather than rendered exactly, grass in soft sage, dirt in honey-warm tones. No team logos, no spectators, no scoreboard. The kid's face is in three-quarter view, joyful but not perfect — a missing tooth would be appropriate. Foreground space at the bottom for headline overlay.

Aspect: 16:9.

### 3. "America's missing rec layer." — `/game/the-missing-rec-layer/`

> A nearly empty soccer field at dusk, viewed from the sideline. One thirteen-year-old in scrimmage gear stands in the middle distance, kicking a ball alone toward an empty net. The bleachers in the foreground are empty except for a single backpack on the bottom row. Sage grass slightly trampled. A single small puddle reflects the warm sky. Quiet, slightly melancholy register, but not sad — observational. The viewer is sitting on those bleachers. Negative space at upper right for headline overlay.

Aspect: 16:9.

## Hero illustrations for high-traffic articles (10 pieces, 4:3)

### 4. "The 90-second rule" — `/drive-home/the-90-second-rule/`

> Close-up: a parent's hands, mid-twenties to mid-forties, holding a steering wheel. Through the windshield, the back of a young child's head visible in a rearview mirror reflection. The dashboard clock shows 4:47 PM. Honey-warm late-afternoon light. The mood is "the next thing I say matters." Negative space upper-right.

### 5. "Night before tryouts" — `/drive-there/night-before-tryouts/`

> A kid's bedroom at night. Cleats, a glove, and a folded jersey laid out on a chair near the bed. Soft lamp light, muted rose tones. The kid is in bed, partially under the covers, awake but settling. A parent figure visible only as a silhouette in the doorway. The framing puts the gear in focus and the parent at the edge — the night before is about preparation, not pressure.

### 6. "What my kid said in the car" — `/drive-home/what-my-kid-said-in-the-car/`

> Backseat of a car, view from the back-passenger window. A nine-year-old looks out the window, profile visible. Their breath fogs the glass slightly. Outside the window, suggestions of a passing neighborhood — soft greens and warm grays. The kid is mid-thought, not crying, not happy, just processing. The image should feel like a held silence.

### 7. "On not making varsity" — `/drive-home/on-not-making-varsity/`

> A teenager (15-17) sits on the front porch of a house in mid-afternoon, headphones in, scrolling on a phone. A backpack is dropped beside them. The light is warm but the framing is slightly low and pulled back, giving the kid space. The viewer is a parent who has just gotten home and is debating whether to walk over.

### 8. "Coaching your own kid in front of the team" — `/game/coaching-your-own-kid-in-front-of-the-team/`

> A coach's back, viewed from behind, kneeling in front of a baseball dugout to talk to a small group of players. The composition emphasizes that we cannot see the coach's face. One of the kids looks faintly familiar to the rest — the coach's own kid. Cream paper background, warm sage grass, terracotta dirt. The mood is the strange-and-specific moment of being two people at once.

### 9. "Your kid is the worst on the team" — `/game/your-kid-is-the-worst-on-the-team/`

> A child sits on the end of a bench in a soccer-field dugout, alone, head down on a water bottle. The rest of the team is loosely suggested in the middle distance, celebrating a goal. The light favors the bench end where the kid is. The composition is honest — not sad but specific. We see the kid's posture more than their face.

### 10. "The night before tryouts (5-7)" — `/drive-there/the-night-before-tryouts-5-to-7/`

> A six-year-old at the kitchen table, hair damp from a bath, eating a small bowl of pasta in pajamas. A parent sits across the table reading a book, not on a phone. The clock on the wall reads 7:50 PM. Soft honey-yellow kitchen light. The mood is calm, ordinary, tomorrow-is-a-day-not-a-judgment.

### 11. "Practice plan parents can read" — `/team-parent/practice-plan-parents-can-read/`

> A clipboard on a bench, viewed from above. The clipboard has a hand-printed practice plan with neat boxes ("warm-up 10 min", "throwing 15 min", etc.). A clip with five waiting kids' nametags clipped to the side. A coffee thermos and a water bottle next to the clipboard. Sage-green metal bench. The aesthetic is "this is the plan; I have the plan; the plan is enough."

### 12. "The hard parent email" — `/team-parent/the-hard-parent-email-script/`

> A parent's hand hovers over a phone showing an email draft. The phone is on the kitchen counter next to a half-empty coffee mug. The email subject visible as "Re: Saturday's game." Warm honey light through a window. The composition holds: don't send it yet. Read it again in the morning.

### 13. "The fair substitution rule" — `/team-parent/the-fair-substitution-rule/`

> A youth soccer field viewed from sideline level. A coach holds a small whiteboard with simple stick figures for a rotation. Two kids in soccer kits stand nearby, listening. The rotation is the point of the image — visible but not over-rendered. The mood is "this is how we make it fair, and I have shown my work."

## Drill library spot illustrations (5 pieces, square)

For the drill index. Each illustration should be readable as a small thumbnail (used at 200×200 in cards) but hold up at 400×400 detail size.

### 17. Cones drill — for any cones-based drill (reusable across many drills)

> Five orange (terracotta) plastic cones arranged on a sage-green field, viewed from a slight elevation. One cone has tipped over slightly. A loose ball sits next to one of the cones. Composition is loose, lived-in, not a perfect grid. Square aspect.

### 18. Tee-ball drill — baseball/softball ages 5-7 drills

> A tee with a ball balanced on top, sitting on cream-colored sand. A small youth bat leans against the tee. A single batter's helmet, oversized for a small head, sits to the side. Square. The mood: the equipment of a six-year-old's sport, observed quietly.

### 19. Soccer dribbling — youth soccer drills

> Three soft-rendered soccer balls of different sizes (size 3, 4, 5) sitting on grass. The grass is sage and slightly textured. Honey light. The composition resembles a still life, not an action shot. Square.

### 20. Whistle still life — for any "game management" or "warm-up" drill

> A coach's whistle on a lanyard, lying on a clipboard. The clipboard has a roster sheet partially visible. A water bottle next to it. Sage-green bench background. Editorial still-life, square.

### 21. Practice huddle — for "culture" drills

> Six pairs of kids' sneakers and cleats, arranged in a loose circle as if their owners are standing in a huddle. The shoes are visible from the knees down — implying a circle of kids without showing their faces. Cream concrete or sage grass. Honey-warm light. Square. The composition is the metaphor: the team is the circle of feet.

## How to ship these to the site

Once images are generated, save them to `/public/illustrations/` with names that match the article slug or the section purpose:

```
/public/illustrations/
  three-drives-one-relationship.jpg
  the-real-job-of-youth-sports.jpg
  the-missing-rec-layer.jpg
  the-90-second-rule.jpg
  drive-home-divider.svg
  …
```

Then in the article frontmatter, add a `hero` field:

```yaml
---
title: "Three drives. *One relationship.*"
hero: /illustrations/three-drives-one-relationship.jpg
heroAlt: "A child in a car after a tee-ball game, seen from the dashboard view."
---
```

The article layout already accepts `hero` and `heroAlt` (from the existing schema). It just needs the file to exist.

For inline section spots (dividers, drill thumbnails), drop the file in `/public/illustrations/` and reference with a regular `<img>` tag in the markdown body or the relevant template.

---

## What NOT to use AI for

- Camp logos (those come from the actual camps when they claim their listing)
- Real product photography (Amazon affiliate links use Amazon's product images, not generated ones)
- Photos of identifiable people (legal + trust risk)
- Anything that needs to look like a screenshot or a chart (use real screenshots / hand-drawn SVG instead)

---

## A note on style consistency

If you generate these one at a time, they will start to drift in style. To keep them coherent, generate them in batches of 3-5 with the brand brief at the top of every session. If the tool supports a "style reference" upload (Midjourney, Firefly), pin the first generated illustration as the style anchor and use it as a reference for everything else.
