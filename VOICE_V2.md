# Voice v2: narrative editorial

The previous voice (matter-of-fact, terse, second-person directives) reads as instructions. The new voice reads as a parent who lived this and wrote it down.

This doc replaces the prior "How we write" guidance for the **articles** collection only. Drills, gear guides, and resource pages keep their utilitarian voice. They are reference material, not stories.

## Hard bans (run this checklist on every article, before and after rewriting)

These rules are non-negotiable. They predate the v2 voice change and they survive it. If a sentence fails any of these checks, fix it. No exceptions.

1. **No em dashes.** Not one. Use a period or a comma. Search the article for the actual em dash character before saving.
2. **No banned words.** The full list lives in `ABOUT ME/anti-ai-writing-guide.md`. The short version: no "delve," "tapestry," "leverage," "robust," "seamless," "pivotal," "navigate" (as a verb), "embrace," "foster," "elevate," "unlock," "myriad," "bustling," "vibrant," "stark," "testament to," "in the realm of," "in today's [anything] world," "it's important to note," "it's worth mentioning," "the key takeaway is," "in conclusion," "ultimately."
3. **No fake-wisdom triplets.** "Not this. Not that. Not the third thing." is the cadence to kill. Use one specific example, not three abstract ones.
4. **No reframe patterns.** "It's not about X. It's about Y." Stop. Just say what it is.
5. **No empowerment closes.** "You've got this." "You're doing great." "Trust yourself." Out.
6. **No "as a parent" / "as parents" hedges.** We are first-person we. We don't need to remind the reader of our credential.
7. **No abstract nouns at the moment a concrete one will work.** Not "the journey of parenthood." It's "Tuesday at 5:25pm in our car."
8. **No "in this piece" / "in this article" / meta-references.** The piece is the piece. Don't talk about it.
9. **No declarative thesis statements at the top.** "This is a piece about X." Out. Open with the moment.
10. **No bullets unless the content is genuinely a list** (a packing list, a ranked checklist of physical items). Story content gets paragraphs.

If the rewrite agent or any future writer can't tell whether something violates these, default to deleting it.

## Four rules for the new voice

### 1. POV: first-person editorial "we"

Every article speaks as the editorial team. A we, not a he or a she, never an I attached to one named writer. The we is plural and kind. It can include the reader naturally ("we've all sent that text"), but its anchor is the parents on this side of the screen who lived these moments first.

> **Old:** Your kid is eight. He played eleven minutes of game time in three weeks.
> **New:** Our kid was eight when this came up. We watched him stand on the sideline for three weeks while everyone else got real minutes.

### 2. Length: 700–1,400 words

Notes still run shorter (700–900) and essays longer (1,000–1,400). Both should feel like a parent who sat down to write something real, not a checklist. If a piece is hitting under 600 words, ask whether the moment got room to breathe.

### 3. Rhythm: paragraphs that breathe, with single-sentence punches for emphasis

Default paragraph length: 4 to 6 sentences. Use single-sentence paragraphs for the line that lands. The rhythm should sound like someone reading aloud, not a slide deck of bullets.

> **Old (one-sentence-per-line cadence):**
> Don't reply for 20 minutes. Get up. Make coffee. Check the calendar. Confirm with your kid whether 5:30 works.
>
> **New (paragraph + punch):**
> The temptation is to reply right away. We've done it. We've sent yes from bed at 7:04 to a Tuesday practice that we couldn't actually make. The coach scrambled at 4pm. Our family ate dinner late. By Thursday we were apologizing for a small thing that didn't need to happen.
>
> Twenty minutes of coffee fixes most of that.

### 4. Specificity: anonymized real-feeling scenes

Open with a moment. A specific car ride, a specific Tuesday text, a specific kitchen-floor meltdown. Anonymize. Never use a real name without permission, never attribute a quote to a named real child. But let the scene be specific enough that a reader who has been there says *yes, that's exactly it.*

> **Old:** Your kid had a hard practice. The car ride home matters.
> **New:** It was a Tuesday. The dog had been alone for nine hours. The piano lesson got skipped. By the time we pulled into the field at 5:25 our kid was already in the car-back-seat slump that means a hard practice is coming. We knew it before he did.

## What "too flowery" means: calibration

After a first sample, the calibration shifted. The voice should sound like a smart parent journaling, not a memoirist. Specific guidance:

- **No theatrical metaphors.** "Constructed a small theatrical production in our head" → "decided the coach was annoyed and our kid would sit Saturday."
- **No literary flourishes.** "Five words and a comma" → "five words." "With a refrigerator in his other hand" → cut.
- **No self-narrating writer voice.** "This is the piece we wish someone had handed us in October" → cut. Just write the piece.
- **No poetic scene padding.** "the cones still in the car," "the late-afternoon light," "in the back-seat slump that means" → cut. State the facts. Let the reader feel the moment without us decorating it.
- **Prefer plain words.** "We had spent the better part of an hour" → "We spent an hour." "We had constructed" → "We had decided."
- **Italic emphasis only for actual quoted content.** Email replies, things kids say, things spouses say. Not for atmospheric effect.

The aim is *journal entry by a smart parent who isn't trying to be a writer*. They tell us what happened, what they were thinking, what they did, what they wish they had done. They don't reach for metaphors.

## What stays from the prior voice rules

- No banned words list violations (no "delve," "tapestry," "leverage," "robust," "seamless," "pivotal," etc.).
- No fake-wisdom triplets ("not this, but this; not that, but the other thing").
- No empowerment closes ("you've got this!").
- No em dashes (use periods or commas).
- No reframe patterns ("It's not about X. It's about Y.").
- Editorial standards from `EDITORIAL_STANDARDS.md` still apply: no gendered defaults, no class assumptions, no moral verdicts on parenting choices, no identity stereotypes.

## What changes from the prior voice rules

- Three-sentence paragraph cap is **lifted** for articles.
- Second-person directive ("Don't text the coach") becomes first-person observation ("We've sent that text. We don't anymore. Here's why.").
- Bullet-style cadence is **out** unless the content is genuinely a list (e.g., a packing list, a ranked checklist).
- Articles can use scene-setting openers up to 100 words long before the first piece of advice lands.

## What about the drills, gear guides, resources?

Unchanged. Those are reference content. Quick scan, find what you need, leave. Voice v2 is for the **articles collection only**, `src/content/articles/`, where the reader is reading for the moment, not the answer.
