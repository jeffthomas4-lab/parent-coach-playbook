## Email 1 — Day 3, before the game

**Subject:** don't ask this the night before tryouts

**Preview text:** The question that feels supportive and does the opposite.

**Body:**

Your kid packed the bag four times tonight. Cleats, water bottle, spare hair tie. You watch them line everything up, then take it apart, then do it again.

Here's the instinct almost every parent has the night before tryouts: ask how they're feeling.

Don't.

The question puts a magnifying glass on the exact thing they're already turning over in their head. It doesn't calm anyone down. It just confirms there's something to be nervous about.

What works instead: be in the room. Fold the towel that came out of the dryer. Watch an episode you've both already seen. Go to bed at the normal time.

## Email 2 — Day 6, the game itself

**Subject:** what youth sports is actually for

**Preview text:** Not recruiting. Not the next roster. Three things, and that's it.

**Body:**

Youth sports has one job, and it's not preparation for college.

The job is three things. Have fun. Learn the rules and how to be a good teammate. Want to come back next year.

That third one is the only metric that matters under twelve. Did they come back? If a kid quits at eight because the coach yelled, that's not a kid problem. If a kid quits at ten because their best friend made the A team and they didn't, that's the program's sorting failure, not the kid's.

About seven percent of high school athletes play any college sport. About two percent play Division I. The kid in your minivan is, almost certainly, in the other ninety-three percent, and that's not a consolation prize. That's the actual population this whole thing is built for.

## Email 3 — Day 9, after the game

**Subject:** the first 90 seconds after a bad game

**Preview text:** What you say at minute one matters more than what you say at minute thirty.

**Body:**

My kid went 0 for 4 last Saturday. I was the assistant coach on his team. I was also his dad. Complicated set of facts to drive home with.

I opened with something I thought was helpful. He didn't talk the rest of the day. By Monday he said he never wanted to play again.

The first ninety seconds after a game decide what the next week looks like in your house. In those ninety seconds your kid is asking one quiet question: is this still a place I want to be. Not the team. The car. You.

Three things work, in this order. Pick a specific moment they had agency in, not a moment that decided the score. Stop talking, just for a mile. Mention something that has nothing to do with the game.

## Email 4 — Day 12, the other parent

**Subject:** the parent two seats away who won't stop yelling

**Preview text:** When it's your call to say something, and when it isn't.

**Body:**

The dad two seats away has been yelling at his own kid in left field for an hour. Other parents are looking at each other.

Most of the time you let it go. It's the other team. Not your kid. Not your call.

But sometimes it's loud enough that your kid is watching it happen, the kids on the field are flinching, and the whole section is uncomfortable. Then it's worth knowing the actual line: if he's yelling at his own kid, that's his problem, and saying something usually escalates him. If he's yelling at the umpire or another team's kids, that's a league-conduct issue, and the umpire is your first call, not him directly.

If your kid noticed, and they always do, the answer is short. He's having a hard time. It's not about you.

## Email 5 — Day 15, the long view

**Subject:** why any of this is worth it

**Preview text:** The last note in this sequence. After this you'll hear from us weekly.

**Body:**

This is the last email in this sequence. After this you'll get our regular note, about once a week.

Here's the zoomed-out version of everything the last four emails covered. The night before, the game, the ninety seconds after, the parent who won't stop yelling: none of it is really about the sport. It's about whether your kid learns the car is a safe place, whether they learn you're on their side whether they went 4 for 4 or 0 for 4, and whether they still want to play next season.

Most of what happens in youth sports gets forgotten by October. What sticks is how the ride home felt.
