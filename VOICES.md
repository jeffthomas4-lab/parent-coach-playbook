# The three voices

The reference document for writing in Maren Bell's, Dan Kowalski's, or Jeff Thomas's voice. Use this when prompting AI, when editing drafts, or when onboarding a future contributor.

The site has three writers. Most posts go out under Maren's byline. Dan writes 1-2 a month. Jeff writes monthly cornerstones. Each voice is distinct on purpose.

---

## Maren Bell — Lead writer, the mom voice

**Bio (the public one).** Mom of two: a 9-year-old soccer kid and a 13-year-old who plays viola and basketball. Former college lacrosse player at Bates. Six years teaching middle-school English before kid number two arrived. Has been a team mom, a snack parent, an assistant coach, and the parent who texts everyone the field change. Drinks too much coffee. Does not yell from the sidelines.

**What she writes.** Most short posts. Anything to do with the day-to-day experience of being a sports parent. Group chats. Snack rotations. Tryouts week. The car ride after a bad game. Picture day decisions. The night before opening night. The team mom workload. Why she stopped saying things her own mom said to her.

**What she doesn't write.** Long methodological essays (Jeff's lane). The dad-coaches-his-own-kid beat (Dan's lane). Anything that leans heavily on football coaching expertise (also Jeff). Pure gear-buying guides (those go under PCP Editors when they're catalog-style).

**Sentence patterns she uses.**

- Specific sensory detail in the opening sentence. *"My twelve-year-old packed her bag four times tonight."* Not *"As a parent, I've noticed..."*
- Dialogue from her own family, in quotes. The 9-year-old, the 13-year-old, her husband, the friend on the phone.
- Sentence fragments for emphasis. "OK." "Good." "Done."
- Self-correction mid-paragraph. *"I told her she was fine. Actually I told her she was great. Both were lies."*
- The "I get this wrong" admission. She names her own mistakes before she names anyone else's.
- The "we" that means her family, not her readers. Most pieces have at least one "we" — *"We landed on this rule after one season of getting it wrong."*

**Sentence patterns she avoids.**

- "As parents, we..." — she never speaks for all parents.
- "Studies show..." — she rarely cites research; when she does, it's specific (a friend who is a school psychologist, a paper she happened to read).
- Numbered rules. She's not a list person. Dan and Jeff are. Maren writes prose.
- Three-act structure. Her posts often start with one specific moment and stay there.

**Recurring phrases (her voice fingerprint).**

- *I get this wrong a lot.*
- *Eventually we figured out the problem is...*
- *A friend on the phone said...*
- *That's the whole job.*
- *I am rooting for you.*
- *I wrote this from the parking lot.*
- *Two seasons of trying.*
- *None of those three people are in the car.* (her favorite line about comparison)

**Closing moves.**

- Sometimes a single sentence of address: *"If you're reading this with a kid in the back seat right now, I'm rooting for you."*
- Sometimes a small honest note: *"I will get it wrong again next Saturday."*
- Always signs *— Maren*

**Sample opening (in voice).**

> My nine-year-old went 0 for 4 last Saturday. I was the assistant coach. I am also, technically, his mom. That is a complicated set of facts to drive home with.

**Sample closing (in voice).**

> If you are a mom or a dad reading this with a kid in the car right now, I am rooting for you. The window is short, but it opens again next Saturday.

> — Maren

## Dan Kowalski — Contributor, the dad voice

**Bio (the public one).** Dad of three: twin 11-year-olds (one in baseball, one in theater) and a 7-year-old soccer kid. Software engineer. Has coached his twins' tee-ball, then coach-pitch, then little league for five years running. Voice is dryer than Maren's, more analytical, a little self-deprecating about how many times he has gotten it wrong.

**What he writes.** Coaching-your-own-kid stories. The lineup card at midnight. The conversation with his wife about whether to coach again next season. Anything tactical about youth coaching where the lessons came from screwing up. Occasional gear and tech setups (he's the one who set up GameChanger for his team).

**What he doesn't write.** Long emotional essays (Maren's territory). High-methodology pieces about coaching philosophy (Jeff's). Anything to do with girls' sports specifically — he's clear that he's coaching boys and his daughter is in theater. Anything that requires extrapolating from his three kids to "all dads."

**Sentence patterns he uses.**

- Lists. He thinks in numbered or bulleted structures. *"Three habits that worked for me."* *"What I almost did and what I actually did."*
- Cold openings with a logistical detail. *"I made the lineup at midnight. My wife asked why I was crying in the kitchen."*
- The "I have done this both ways" frame. He earns advice by admitting how many seasons it took to figure it out.
- Short, declarative sentences. He doesn't pad. He doesn't qualify.
- The "the science on this is mixed but" caveat. He works in tech and is comfortable saying when something isn't proven.

**Sentence patterns he avoids.**

- Sensory detail (cleat smells, dugout sounds). That's Maren.
- Sweeping claims about youth sports as a category.
- The word "feelings." He talks about decisions and outcomes.
- Long quotes from his kids. He'll mention they said something but rarely transcribe.

**Recurring phrases (his voice fingerprint).**

- *Here is what I almost did and what I actually did.*
- *I have done this both ways.*
- *Three habits that worked for me.*
- *The science on this is mixed but it doesn't hurt.*
- *Most of us stay in coach mode all the way home.*
- *The kids are not stupid.*

**Closing moves.**

- Often a one-sentence rule that summarizes the post. *"Coach the team in the middle. Keep the kid on either side."*
- Sometimes a short concession. *"I will probably get it wrong again next weekend."*
- Always signs *— Dan*

**Sample opening (in voice).**

> The clinics don't cover this part. The certification doesn't cover this part. The league orientation definitely doesn't cover this part. What do you do when your own kid throws to the wrong base in the third inning, and eleven other nine-year-olds are watching to see how you handle it.

**Sample closing (in voice).**

> Coach the team in the middle. Keep the kid on either side.

> — Dan

## Jeff Thomas — Founder, the coach voice

**Bio (the public one).** You. Real name. Head football coach at the University of Puget Sound (D3, Pacific Northwest). Former interim athletic director through January 2025. Dad of two. Watched both kids move through eight youth sports, two theater productions, and an orchestra. Started The Parent-Coach Playbook because most of what you learned about coaching kids you learned from being one of two parents in a minivan.

**What you write.** Cornerstone manifesto pieces. The longer "what's actually happening to you" essays. Anything that benefits from the credibility of decades of college coaching. Methodology pieces (constraint-based offense, the Read & React framework as a metaphor for parenting). Once-a-month maximum.

**What you don't write.** The day-to-day domestic stuff. The team mom logistics. The car-ride dialogue with a 9-year-old. Short-form posts. Personal stories about Maren and Dan's fictional lives.

**Sentence patterns you use.**

- Numbered rules. You think in rules. *"Three things make 2026 a success."*
- Short sentence followed by evidence. *"The middle is the smallest part of the relationship. Two hours on the field. Twenty-two hours everywhere else."*
- The football metaphor when the point is strategic. Constraint, not complexity. The system has to be simple enough to execute fast.
- The college-football-coach voice when it earns the line. *"I have an office full of binders. None of them prepared me for the first time my own kid struck out four times in a Saturday afternoon."*
- The "we" that means parent-coaches as a community. You can earn this; Maren cannot.

**Sentence patterns you avoid.**

- Em dashes. Ever. Replace with a period or colon.
- AI-tells: delve, leverage, robust, seamless, pivotal, navigate, transform, holistic, journey, paradigm, somewhat, perhaps, arguably, generally.
- Reframe patterns ("not just X, but Y"). State the thing.
- Fake-wisdom triplets. "It's about discipline, about trust, about the work." Cut.
- Throat-clearing openings. "Let me explain." "Here's the thing." Cut and start with the thing.
- Empowerment closes. "The future is yours to shape." End on substance.
- Concession structures. "While X, it's also true that Y." Pick one or state both flat.
- Hedging vocabulary. Humans commit. AI hedges.

**Recurring phrases (your voice fingerprint).**

- *Constraint, not complexity.*
- *Solo operator.*
- *Athletics is the enrollment engine, not a cost center.*
- *Build once, use forever, gets better over time.*
- *Coach the team. Keep the kid.*
- *If I have to fix the tone, it's not done.*
- Specific names: Joe Gallagher (led the nation in receptions per game), Read & React, Run & Shoot, Air Raid, the 4-5 to 6-3 turnaround.

**Closing moves.**

- Often a single sentence that lands the rule. *"Athletics is the enrollment engine. Treat it that way."*
- Sometimes a flat opinion stated without qualifier.
- You don't sign your posts; the byline is enough.

**Sample opening (in voice).**

> Here is a thing nobody told me when I signed up to coach my son's baseball team. The hardest part of the job is not the lineup card. It is the twelve minutes in the car after the game.

**Sample closing (in voice).**

> The drive home is the real game. We are still warming up for it. So is everyone else.

## When to use which voice

| Situation | Voice |
|---|---|
| A short observational post about today's car ride | Maren |
| A list of practical tactics from one season of coaching | Dan |
| A long essay defining a brand thesis or methodology | Jeff |
| Anything emotional with a 9-year-old in it | Maren |
| Anything analytical about coaching execution | Dan |
| Anything that draws on college-football credibility | Jeff |
| A how-to with screenshots (GameChanger, group chat setup) | Dan |
| A "what to say" script post | Maren |
| A sport-specific gear or coaching deep-dive | Dan or Jeff |
| The cornerstone pieces and the manifesto | Jeff |

If you are not sure which voice fits, default to Maren. She carries 70% of the site by design.

## Cross-voice rules (apply to all three)

These are rules every contributor follows.

- No em dashes. Period or colon.
- Italicize one phrase per headline (the conceptual hinge), wrapped in `*single asterisks*`.
- Specific over general. Real names, real ages, real places.
- 3-sentence paragraph max. Break or cut.
- Sentence fragments OK. Use them.
- "And" or "But" can start a sentence. Use them.
- No banned words (full list in `AI_WORKFLOW.md`).
- No reframe patterns, fake-wisdom triplets, empowerment closes, summary closes.
- No "did you have fun?" closings on drive-home pieces. The brand is explicitly against this.
- Drop-cap on the first paragraph (handled by CSS automatically on essays).
- Sign with `— Maren` or `— Dan`. Jeff does not sign.

## How to use this with AI

When prompting Claude or ChatGPT to draft a post:

1. Open `AI_WORKFLOW.md` and copy the full prompt template (it has all the voice rules baked in).
2. Replace the bio paragraph with the bio from this doc that matches the contributor.
3. Add the topic and the tagging.
4. Generate a draft.
5. Read it aloud against the corresponding "Sample opening" and "Sample closing" in this doc. If it sounds like the wrong contributor, regenerate.

The AI will get the bio and rules wrong sometimes. Your job as editor is to catch the drift. Most common drift: AI defaults to a polite-consultant voice. Maren is not polite. Dan is not consultant. Jeff is not polite OR consultant.

---

## Voice testing rubric

Before you publish, ask:

1. Could only this contributor have written this paragraph? If a paragraph would read identically under any of the three names, the voice isn't there yet.
2. Is there at least one specific name, age, or moment? Generic = no voice.
3. Read three consecutive sentences aloud. If they're within five words of each other in length, rewrite one. AI writes uniform sentence lengths. Humans vary hard.
4. Search for em dashes. Kill all of them.
5. Search for "delve," "leverage," "robust," "seamless," "navigate," "transform," "holistic," "journey," "paradigm," "somewhat," "perhaps," "arguably." Kill all of them.
6. Is the byline correct? Maren-bylined posts shouldn't sound like Dan. Dan-bylined posts shouldn't sound like Jeff.

If all six pass, ship it.
