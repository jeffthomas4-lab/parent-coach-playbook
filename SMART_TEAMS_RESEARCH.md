# Smart-Teams research: pick up the mantel

**Date:** May 9, 2026
**Source:** smart-teams.org (microsite for the retired MomsTEAM/MomsTEAM Institute), plus archival pages on momsteam.com via search. Wikipedia for organizational background.
**Purpose:** Map what MomsTEAM covered across 25 years of work. Identify where Parent Coach Playbook already covers the same ground, where the gaps are, and where PCP can take the mantel further than MomsTEAM ever could.

---

## 1. What MomsTEAM was

Founded 2000 by Brooke de Lench. Became 501(c)(3) MomsTEAM Youth Sports Safety Institute in 2013. UNICEF Pioneer Organization for U.S. Implementation of International Safeguards for Children in Sport (2015). PBS film *The Smartest Team: Making High School Football Safer* (2013). Built the largest concussion safety library on the internet (400+ articles in that one channel alone).

Headquarters: Concord, MA. Mostly volunteer expert contributor model. Donor-funded with occasional sponsor partnerships (Schutt Sports, Riddell, etc.). Founder is now retiring; live site is being archived. A curated subset is moving to Smart-Teams.org as the institutional home.

This is the organization the Strategic Audit identified as creating the largest content-authority vacuum in the youth sports parent space. Their exit is PCP's biggest single market opportunity.

## 5. Where PCP can take the mantel further than MomsTEAM ever could

This is the strategic answer, not the tactical one. MomsTEAM did extraordinary work but was structurally limited. PCP can take five specific positions where the structural shape of the operation gives it an advantage.

### A. Voice that travels
MomsTEAM's voice was institutional and credentialed. The byline on most articles read like a clinical journal: "Eating disorders in athletes: warning signs and clinical considerations." That voice earned authority but did not travel through Facebook groups, parent text chains, or email forwards. PCP's peer-to-peer voice does. The same content under a "what to actually do when your daughter stops eating after gymnastics practice" headline reaches 10x the parents.

The mantel-pickup move: write every piece in PCP's voice. Cite the same evidence. Reach the parent who would never click an Aspen Project Play link.

### B. Tools, not just articles
MomsTEAM published articles. PCP publishes tools that operate on the parent's actual situation. Cost calculator. Camps directory. Age pathways. Season calendars. Pendulum quiz. Each tool is a backlink magnet, an email signup machine, and a decision aid in one. MomsTEAM never built tooling; the budget was always pointed at content.

The mantel-pickup move: every gap in section 4 above gets a tool wherever a tool will work. The cardiac safety pillar gets an "AED finder" tool that surfaces the AED locations at the user's kid's primary fields (sourced from the camps directory + community submissions). The female athlete health pillar gets a "RED-S risk screener" decision aid. The lightning safety pillar gets a "league policy template" downloadable for parents to bring to their board. Tools beat articles for shareability and for SEO.

### C. Commercial alignment that funds the work
MomsTEAM operated as a non-profit with intermittent donor funding. When the funding lulled, the publishing rate dropped. PCP's affiliate-and-display model means the production engine self-funds. A 5-piece cardiac safety pillar generates $0 from MomsTEAM's model. The same pillar on PCP generates affiliate revenue from every chest protector pick, every AED reference (where applicable), every mouthguard recommendation, plus ad RPM on the traffic. Commercial alignment lets PCP publish the safety content non-profits cannot afford to maintain.

The mantel-pickup move: every safety pillar pays for itself. The cardiac pillar funds the AED finder tool's hosting. The female athlete pillar's sports-bra What to Buy guide funds the RED-S screener tool. The hazing pillar funds itself through linked SafeSport-related books on Bookshop.org. None of this is mercenary; it is the discipline that keeps the lights on.

### D. Live data, not legacy data
MomsTEAM's archive is largely static. The concussion microsite has 400 articles, but the most recent ones are from 2018-2020. The science has moved. PCP can publish what is current, refresh the body content on the existing 18-month cycle (per the Article Refresh Plan in §9.5 of the business plan), and keep pace with new research. This matters most in cardiac safety (new AED studies and sport-specific risk data emerge regularly), in concussion (the protocols have updated three times in the past five years), and in female athlete health (the RED-S framework has supplanted the triad and most older content does not reflect this).

The mantel-pickup move: each safety pillar gets put on the refresh schedule from day one. Every piece carries a "last reviewed" date. Citations link to the most recent governing-body guidance.

### E. Cross-pollination with the rest of the site
MomsTEAM's safety content lived in a silo. The reader had to hunt for it. PCP can wire safety content into the contexts where parents actually need it: the gear guide for lacrosse links to the chest protector buying piece, which links to the commotio cordis decision, which links to the AED finder tool, which links to the league communications template. One reader trajectory through the site collects 6 pieces of related content and 3 lead-magnet downloads.

The mantel-pickup move: every gap-filling piece in section 4 gets explicit cross-links to existing PCP content. The internal-link discipline that the strategic audit already named as a strength becomes the structural advantage that turns visitor sessions into multi-page reader trajectories.

## 8. Risks of the pickup strategy

Three to flag honestly.

**Liability surface.** Safety content carries higher liability exposure than essay content. A parent who follows PCP's commotio cordis advice and a child still dies has a claim PCP needs insurance against. The business plan §12.4 already scopes the insurance build (GL + E&O by month 8-10). The safety pillar accelerates the urgency of that quote. Get insurance in place before the cardiac pieces ship.

**Authority signal.** PCP is one coach with a real-but-narrow medical-adjacent credentialing footprint. MomsTEAM had Brooke de Lench's 25 years and a stable of MD contributors. The pieces in the 30-piece backlog must cite governing-body guidance (NOCSAE, NFHS, AAP, AMSSM, US Lacrosse, USA Hockey, USA Baseball, US Soccer Foundation) rather than presenting PCP as the medical authority. PCP is the parent-facing translator. The medical authority is the cited body.

**Voice drift risk.** Safety content has a strong pull toward institutional register because of the seriousness of the topic. Hold the line. Every safety piece passes the same Anti AI Writing checklist as every essay. "What to do when your kid takes a chest hit" beats "Considerations for parental response to chest impact in pediatric athletes" every time, even when the underlying content is identical. The voice is what makes PCP the place this content actually gets read.

MomsTEAM built the largest youth-sports parent safety library on the internet. The site is now archived. The audience that read it is now without an active editorial home in this category. PCP can become that home in 6-9 months by shipping the 30-piece backlog above.

The work is not derivative. The content cites the same governing bodies and reflects the same evidence base, but it is rewritten in PCP's voice, paired with PCP's tools, distributed through PCP's channels, and self-funded through PCP's commercial model.

The mantel is sitting on the field. Pick it up.
