# Editorial audit

A direct review. What you have. What's missing if the goal is to be the unmistakable home for parents and parent-coaches in youth sports. Ranked by how much each gap matters.

---

## Where you stand today

Content live or scaffolded:

- **11 articles.** Two cornerstones, nine notes.
- **24 What to Buy guides.** Every major youth sport plus the performing arts.
- **24 sizing companion pages.** One per buy guide.
- **14 Team Parent resources.** Communication, practice, game day, fundraising, photos, volunteering, travel, tech setup.
- **1 coaching tip in a brand-new drill library.** The cones diagram with positional placement.
- **1 National Organizations directory** with 30+ governing bodies.
- **2 lead magnets** (Drive Home Playbook PDF, Practice Plan Template PDF).
- **Disclosure, About, Newsletter archive, Reads index, Start Here, Search.**
- **A camps repository** with the spec done and the build prompt ready for next session.

That is enough to ship and start ranking. It is not enough to own the category.

---

## What the home for youth sports requires

To be unmistakably the place parents land when they need help, the site needs four things at once.

1. **Volume.** Sites that own a category have 300-1,000 evergreen pieces. We have 49 content pieces total.
2. **Authority.** Sources, citations, clinical experts, governing-body partnerships, original research.
3. **Utility tools.** Lead magnets, calculators, planners, templates that are referenced again and again.
4. **Community.** A place where readers send questions, see other parents like them, and feel like they belong.

We have one of these (utility, partly). The other three are starter-level.

## The gaps, ranked

### 1. Volume of evergreen content (highest impact)

49 pieces is a respectable monthly publication. It is one or two percent of what dominant category sites carry. To get serious about ranking on Google for "youth baseball glove size by age" or "what to say to your kid after a bad game," we need 5-10x what we have.

Realistic 12-month target: 200 pieces total. That means 2-3 short reads per week sustained, plus monthly cornerstones, plus the drill library buildout. It is achievable with the AI-assisted workflow already documented.

The highest-leverage 50 pieces to add next, in order:

- **A drill per sport per age band.** Baseball ages 5-7, ages 8-10, ages 11-12, ages 13-14. Same for softball, soccer, basketball, football, hockey, lacrosse, volleyball. Twenty-eight drill-library entries that would make the coaching-tips section actually useful.
- **A "best youth [equipment] for [age]" piece per sport.** Five-year-old baseball gloves. Ten-year-old soccer cleats. Eight-year-old basketball shoes. These are the queries that drive Amazon affiliate revenue and they are completely empty right now.
- **A position guide per team sport.** What does a six-year-old shortstop actually do. What does a soccer fullback do for an eight-year-old. Right field for ages 5-7. Twenty-five of these.
- **The hard moments series.** When your kid quits mid-season. When your kid is the worst on the team. When your kid is the best. When the coach yells at your kid. When the parent on your team is a problem. We have stubs for some. They need to be written.
- **Season-by-season checklists per sport.** Pre-season tryout week. First game. Mid-season tune-up. Playoffs. Off-season. Twelve sports times five season phases is sixty pieces, but four or five well-written ones per sport is enough to start.

### 2. The coaching-tips drill library is empty

We built the architecture and shipped one entry. To be a coaching destination, we need 100+ drills. The differentiator: every drill is filterable by sport, age, focus, and equipment-needed.

A single drill takes 15-30 minutes to write once the format is set. A weekly cadence of two new drills hits 100 in a year.

Priority entries to seed the library:

- **Five tee-ball drills.** Cone positions (live), running the bases, fielding ground balls, throwing across the diamond, pitching machine intro.
- **Five soccer drills for ages 5-8.** Sharks-and-minnows, red-light-green-light with the ball, gates dribbling, four-corner passing, the no-bunching cones drill.
- **Five basketball drills for ages 9-11.** The two-line layup, the chair-passing drill, the rebound triangle, the closeout, the help-and-recover.
- **Five baseball drills for ages 8-10.** Tee work in the cage, soft toss, four-corners, the relay drill, situational base running.
- **Five practice plans by sport and age.** Not drills: full Tuesday-night plans that string the drills together.

That is 25 entries to start. The library starts to feel real at fifty.

### 3. Lead magnets

Two PDFs is a starter. Sites that build email lists in this niche have 8-15 free downloads. Each one is a separate landing page that ranks on a separate keyword.

What to ship next, in order of how much demand exists:

- **The pre-game pocket card.** Glove-box-sized, laminate-able, three things to say before each game. Already in the roadmap.
- **The tryouts evaluation rubric.** A printable scorecard the coach uses during tryouts.
- **The season-opener parent meeting agenda + script.** A one-page agenda plus a verbatim script for the meeting.
- **The snack-signup template.** A Google Sheets template with rotation logic.
- **The drill-rotation template.** A practice planning spreadsheet.
- **The lineup spreadsheet.** Inning-by-inning fair rotation that's auditable.
- **The fundraising calculator.** Plug in roster size, target dollars, get a recommended approach.
- **The end-of-season banquet checklist.**
- **The hotel-block planner.**
- **The travel-tournament packing list as a real PDF.**

Eight more PDFs, each with its own landing page, would ten-x the email signup rate.

### 4. Authority and source citations

Right now the site has plain-English advice. To be authoritative, every factual claim needs a source readers can verify. Specifically:

- Equipment recommendations: link to the governing body's certification requirements (USA Baseball USABat, NOCSAE for helmets, HECC for hockey, NOCSAE ND200 for lacrosse chest protectors). Some guides have this. Most do not.
- Health and safety: link to the CDC, AAP, SafeSport. Currently absent in pieces about concussion risk, return-to-play, mental health.
- Statistics: the "seven percent of high-school athletes play in college" claim is true but unsourced. Cite NCAA recruiting fact sheets. Cite Aspen Institute Project Play.
- Drills: cite the coaching certification courses and the books drills come from when relevant.

Authority comes from being citable. Citable comes from citing.

### 5. Original research

The single biggest unlock to be THE home for youth sports is to publish something nobody else has. Examples:

- An annual reader survey: "What did parents spend on youth sports this year?" Twenty questions. Send to the email list. Publish the results in March.
- A regional camp directory by quality (the camps build, when it ships).
- A "what parents are searching for" report, pulled from the site's own search bar after instrumented for three months.
- A drill inventory: "How many publicly-available youth coaching drills exist for [sport] [age]?" Build the count. Publish.

Pick one. Ship it once a year. Become the source other people cite.

### 6. Topics with no coverage at all

Every one of these is a question parents ask. Every one has zero pieces on the site:

- **Nutrition and hydration for youth athletes.** Three pieces, by sport intensity.
- **Sleep, recovery, and the over-scheduled kid.**
- **Injury prevention beyond protective gear.** Stretching, warm-ups, single-sport specialization risks.
- **Return-to-play protocols.** What to do after a sprain, after a concussion, after a broken bone.
- **The female-athlete-specific guide.** Period and performance, RED-S, the under-eaten teenage athlete.
- **Adaptive sports and kids with disabilities.** Resources, leagues, getting started.
- **The multi-kid family schedule problem.** Two siblings, three sports, two cars, one Saturday.
- **Divorced and stepparent dynamics.** Who carpools to which game. The rotating-schedule problem.
- **NIL at the high-school level.** This is a real thing now. Nobody under thirty knows what to tell parents.
- **Specialization vs multi-sport.** We have stubs. Needs a cornerstone.
- **The coach's-kid dynamics series.** We have it queued. Five cornerstones.

That is fifteen to twenty cornerstones of topical real estate sitting empty. Whichever site claims it first owns it for a long time.

### 7. Image and visual strategy

The site is text. Modern reading audiences expect images. Three options:

- **Curated stock.** Unsplash with a strict editorial filter (no posed-family-on-bleachers shots). Free.
- **Commissioned illustration.** A single illustrator across the brand for $1,500-3,000 of starter art. Distinctive, durable.
- **Hand-drawn diagrams in the editorial style.** SVG illustrations that match the typography (the cones diagram is one of these). Cheap, on-brand.

Recommended path: option 3 plus option 1 sparingly. Twenty SVG diagrams across the next twelve months would give every cornerstone a visual anchor. The cones diagram is the proof of concept.

### 8. Internal linking and "see also"

Every article should end with a "see also" block linking to three related pieces. Right now `RelatedArticles` exists but only links by phase. It should link by sport, by age, by topic, by season phase.

This is the single easiest SEO lift available. A weekend's work.

### 9. Comparison and best-of content

This is what Google rewards in 2026. Current site: zero comparison pieces. Examples to add:

- **GameChanger vs TeamSnap vs Stack Sports.** Which youth-sports app for which family.
- **Wilson A2000 vs Rawlings Heart of the Hide for ages 9-12.** The honest comparison.
- **Best youth baseball cleats under $50 for the 2026 season.**
- **Year-tagged "best of" lists.** "Best youth football helmets 2026, NOCSAE-rated."

These pieces are search-optimized by structure. They double as affiliate revenue. They are not the soul of the brand. They are the engine that funds the soul.

### 10. Community and engagement

Right now the only reader interaction is "reply to the email." Three things would change the engagement profile:

- **A reader letters mailbag.** Once a month, a piece that answers three reader questions in editorial voice. Names removed for privacy. The questions become content.
- **Comments on articles.** Tied to email subscribers. Light moderation. Currently absent.
- **A "submit your situation" form.** A parent in a hard moment writes in. We respond privately. Some get adapted into pieces. Builds the editorial pipeline.

Community is what makes a site a destination instead of a search result.

### 11. Trust signals

Things that build credibility on the homepage and About:

- "As cited by" or "As linked from" badges (governing bodies, school districts, leagues).
- Newsletter subscriber count once it crosses 1,000.
- A press mentions strip on the About page once we have any.
- Editorial standards page in deeper detail than the current About copy.
- A "How we research" page that lays out the methodology.

These compound. Start the press-mentions list with whoever links to us first.

### 12. Tools and calculators

Calculators rank on long-tail searches and earn affiliate revenue. None exist on the site yet. Highest-ROI builds:

- **Bat drop-weight calculator.** Input age, league, get the legal drop weight.
- **Cleat / shoe size converter.** Sport-specific (hockey skates run 1-1.5 sizes smaller, etc.).
- **Annual youth-sports cost calculator.** Input sport, level, region, get the realistic annual cost. This piece would go viral.
- **Practice planner.** Drag and drop drills onto a 75-minute time block.
- **Recruiting reality calculator.** Input GPA, sport, position, level, get the honest probability of a college roster spot.

Build one. Ship it. See if it draws traffic. If yes, build the rest.

### 13. The mobile experience

Untested. Most parent-coach traffic will be from a phone in a parking lot. The site needs a real mobile pass: nav menu, dropdowns, article reading flow, search, sign-up form. Probably one weekend of polish.

### 14. The camps directory

Specced and ready to build. The build prompt is sitting in `CAMPS_BUILD_PROMPT.md`. Once shipped and seeded with 50-100 PNW camps, this becomes a real differentiator. No other parent-facing youth-sports site has a verified camp directory.

### 15. Long-tail SEO research

The site needs a keyword inventory. Tools like Ahrefs or Semrush ($99/month for Ahrefs Lite) reveal which terms have search volume and low competition. Without that data, we are writing the right things in the wrong order.

A one-time keyword audit (or a contracted SEO consultant for $1,500-3,000) would give a six-month editorial calendar that maps directly to organic traffic.

## The three things that would make this unmistakably the home

If I had to pick three changes, in order:

1. **A live, verified camp directory.** Nobody has it. We are building it. Once it lives, we own a slice of search nobody can take.
2. **A 100-entry drill library, filterable.** Coaches search for drills constantly and the existing options (mojo, Stack, individual coach blogs) are scattered and uncurated.
3. **An annual "state of youth sports" reader survey.** Twenty questions, publishable as a report, pickable up by the press. Becomes the citation everyone uses.

Each one is six to twelve weeks of focused work. All three together are the difference between a small site and a category-defining one.

## What would unblock the rest

The single biggest unblocker is engineering time being spent on the highest-leverage thing. Right now: ship the camps repo. After camps: drill library. After drill library: lead magnets and SEO.

The second biggest unblocker is the writing cadence. Two short reads a week is the minimum. The AI-assisted workflow already documented makes that doable in six to nine hours of editorial time per week. Tighter cadences are possible if a second editor joins.

The third biggest unblocker is image strategy. The site is text-only and that puts a ceiling on engagement. A single decision (stock, commissioned, or hand-drawn) and a one-time investment unlocks the rest.

Everything else flows from those three.
